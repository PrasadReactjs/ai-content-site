Teachable vs Udemy: Which Platform is Right for Your Online Course?

If you’re an educator, entrepreneur, or subject-matter expert looking to share your knowledge online, you’ve likely encountered two of the biggest names in the space: Teachable and Udemy. Both platforms allow you to create and sell courses, but they cater to very different goals, audiences, and business models. In this comprehensive comparison, we’ll break down pricing, features, audience reach, and monetization options—so you can decide which platform aligns with your teaching style and income ambitions. Whether you’re just starting out or scaling an existing business, understanding the teachable vs udemy debate is essential for making the right choice.

Overview: Two Different Philosophies

Before diving into specifics, it’s important to grasp the fundamental difference between these platforms. Udemy operates as a massive online marketplace. You, as the instructor, upload your course to their catalog, and Udemy handles marketing, sales, and customer acquisition—but also takes a significant cut of your revenue. Teachable, on the other hand, is a self-hosted platform. You build your own school, control your brand, set your own prices, and retain a much higher percentage of every sale. You are responsible for driving your own traffic and sales.

This core distinction influences everything from pricing to audience control. Let’s explore each area in detail.

Pricing: What You Pay and What You Keep

Udemy’s Pricing Model

Udemy is free to join. There are no monthly subscription fees for instructors. However, the revenue split is heavily weighted in Udemy’s favor—unless you generate sales through your own marketing efforts.

  • Organic sales (Udemy-driven): Udemy takes 63% of the course price. You keep 37%.
  • Instructor-driven sales (via your coupon or link): You keep 97% of the sale, and Udemy takes just 3%.
  • Udemy Business (corporate subscriptions): You earn a share of a revenue pool based on student watch time, which can be unpredictable.

Udemy frequently runs deep discounts (courses often sell for $10–$20), which can limit your per-course earnings. The upside is that you don’t need to invest in ads or a website to get started.

Teachable’s Pricing Model

Teachable charges a monthly subscription fee, but you keep nearly all of your revenue. Plans start at around $39/month (Basic) and go up to $119/month (Pro) or $299/month (Business). Transaction fees apply on lower-tier plans.

  • Free Plan: $0/month, but Teachable takes a $1 + 10% transaction fee per sale.
  • Basic Plan ($39/month): 5% transaction fee. No per-sale fee.
  • Pro Plan ($119/month): No transaction fees. Includes advanced features like course completion certificates and affiliate marketing.
  • Business Plan ($299/month): No transaction fees. Includes bulk student imports, custom user roles, and priority support.

If you sell high-ticket courses (e.g., $200+), the monthly subscription quickly pays for itself. For lower-priced courses, the transaction fees can eat into margins.

Which Pricing is Better?

If you have zero budget and want to test the waters, Udemy’s free model is attractive. But if you plan to build a sustainable business and keep 80–97% of your revenue, Teachable wins on long-term profitability—especially once you have a steady stream of students.

Features: Course Creation and Student Management

Teachable Features

Teachable is built for creators who want full control. Key features include:

  • Drag-and-drop course builder: Easily organize lectures, quizzes, and assignments.
  • Customizable school branding: Use your own domain, logo, and color scheme.
  • Multimedia support: Upload video, audio, PDFs, and text lessons.
  • Integrated payment gateways: Accept credit cards, PayPal, and Apple Pay.
  • Email marketing integration: Connect with Mailchimp, ConvertKit, or ActiveCampaign.
  • Affiliate program management: Recruit affiliates and pay commissions automatically.
  • Course completion certificates: Boost student motivation and credibility.
  • Drip content and scheduling: Release lessons over time to keep students engaged.

Udemy Features

Udemy’s features are focused on ease of use and scale. Key features include:

  • Simple course uploader: Add video lectures, articles, and resources.
  • Built-in marketplace: Your course is instantly listed among millions of students.
  • Automated marketing: Udemy runs promotions, email campaigns, and search ads for you.
  • Q&A and student forums: Engage with students directly within the platform.
  • Course analytics: Track enrollments, revenue, and student engagement.
  • Mobile app: Students can learn on the go via Udemy’s app.
  • Closed captioning and transcripts: Improve accessibility and SEO.

Feature Comparison Verdict

Teachable offers more flexibility, branding control, and advanced monetization tools (like affiliates and subscriptions). Udemy is simpler but locks you into their ecosystem. If you value ownership and customization, Teachable is the clear winner. If you just want to publish a course quickly and let Udemy do the heavy lifting, Udemy’s features are sufficient.

Audience Reach: Who Will Find Your Course?

Udemy’s Massive Built-in Audience

Udemy has over 50 million students worldwide. This is its biggest selling point. You don’t need to build an audience from scratch—your course can be discovered by students searching for topics like “Python programming,” “Digital marketing,” or “Yoga for beginners.” Udemy also invests heavily in Google Ads and affiliate partnerships to drive traffic.

However, the downside is intense competition. With thousands of courses in popular categories, standing out requires high ratings, professional production quality, and constant updates. Also, you have no direct access to student emails—Udemy owns the student relationship.

Teachable’s Self-Taught Audience

Teachable does not provide a built-in marketplace. You must bring your own audience through social media, email lists, SEO, paid ads, or partnerships. This is a steeper climb, especially for beginners.

But the trade-off is significant: you own your student data. You can email them directly, upsell other courses, and build a community. Over time, this leads to higher lifetime customer value and lower dependency on a third-party platform.

Which Platform Reaches More People?

In the short term, Udemy gives you instant access to a massive audience. In the long term, Teachable allows you to build a loyal, owned audience that you can monetize repeatedly. If you have no existing followers, Udemy can jumpstart your career. If you already have a blog, YouTube channel, or email list, Teachable will maximize your earnings per student.

Monetization Options: How You Make Money

Udemy Monetization

  • One-time course sales: Students pay per course (often at discounted prices).
  • Udemy Business: Your course can be included in corporate subscriptions, earning you a share of a revenue pool.
  • No subscription or membership options: Udemy does not support recurring billing for instructors.
  • No affiliate program for instructors: You cannot set up your own affiliate system—Udemy manages its own.

Your income on Udemy is heavily influenced by the platform’s pricing and promotion strategy. Most courses sell for $10–$20, so you need high volume to make significant money. A course with 1,000 sales at $15 (instructor-driven) would net you about $14,550. The same course sold via Udemy’s organic traffic would net only $5,550.
