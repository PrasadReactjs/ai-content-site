Teachable vs Udemy: Which Online Course Platform Is Right for You?

If you’re looking to turn your expertise into a profitable online course, you’ve probably come across two of the biggest names in the industry: Teachable and Udemy. Both platforms allow you to create and sell courses, but they serve very different audiences and business models. In this comprehensive comparison, we’ll break down the key differences in pricing, features, audience reach, and monetization options to help you decide which platform aligns with your goals.

Whether you’re a seasoned instructor or just starting out, understanding the nuances of Teachable vs Udemy is critical to maximizing your earning potential. Let’s dive in.

1. Pricing: What You Pay (and What You Keep)

Teachable Pricing

Teachable operates on a subscription-based model with three main plans:

  • Free Plan: $0/month + 5% transaction fee per sale. Limited features (no coupons, no custom domain).
  • Basic Plan: $39/month (billed annually) + 5% transaction fee. Includes coupons, email marketing, and basic quizzes.
  • Pro Plan: $119/month (billed annually) + 0% transaction fees. Advanced features like course bundles, affiliate marketing, and live chat.
  • Business Plan: $299/month (billed annually) for up to 5 admin users and bulk student imports.

With Teachable, you keep the majority of your revenue after the subscription and transaction fees. This is ideal if you’re building a premium brand with high-ticket courses.

Udemy Pricing

Udemy is completely free for instructors to join and upload courses. However, the revenue split is heavily weighted toward Udemy:

  • Organic Sales: You earn 97% of revenue when a student purchases through your personal coupon or referral link (after a $1 processing fee per sale).
  • Udemy Marketplace Sales: You earn only 3% of revenue when a student discovers and buys your course directly through Udemy’s search or promotions.

Because Udemy frequently runs deep discounts (e.g., courses for $12.99), your per-course earnings can be very low unless you drive your own traffic.

Pricing Verdict

If you want predictable costs and high margins per sale, Teachable wins. If you prefer zero upfront cost and are willing to trade a large revenue share for exposure, Udemy might be tempting—but your actual take-home pay will likely be much lower.

2. Features: Control, Customization, and Tools

Teachable Features

Teachable is designed for creators who want full control over their brand and student experience:

  • Customizable school: Full white-labeling, custom domain, and CSS/HTML editing (on Pro plan).
  • Course creation: Drag-and-drop builder, video, text, quizzes, and downloadable resources.
  • Marketing tools: Built-in email marketing, coupons, affiliate program, and one-click upsells.
  • Student management: Detailed analytics, student groups, and course completion certificates.
  • Payment processing: Integration with Stripe (credit cards) and PayPal (select countries).
  • Integrations: Zapier, Mailchimp, ConvertKit, and many other third-party tools.

Udemy Features

Udemy offers a streamlined but less flexible experience:

  • Course creation: Simple video upload, text lectures, quizzes, and coding exercises.
  • Built-in audience: Udemy has over 60 million students worldwide—your course gets instant exposure.
  • Marketing: Udemy runs promotions and ads for your course, but you have little control over pricing or timing.
  • Student analytics: Basic dashboard showing enrollments, reviews, and revenue.
  • Certificates: Udemy issues completion certificates (but less customizable than Teachable).
  • Mobile app: Students can learn on the go via Udemy’s app.

Features Verdict

Teachable offers far more control, customization, and marketing firepower—perfect for building a sustainable online business. Udemy is simpler and quicker to launch, but you sacrifice branding and pricing control.

3. Audience Reach: Who Will Find Your Course?

Udemy’s Massive Marketplace

Udemy’s biggest draw is its enormous built-in audience. With tens of millions of learners browsing for topics ranging from Python programming to watercolor painting, your course can get discovered without any marketing effort on your part. However, the competition is fierce—over 200,000 courses are already on the platform. To stand out, you need a high-quality course with strong reviews and a niche topic.

Udemy also uses aggressive SEO and paid ads to drive traffic to its marketplace, which benefits instructors indirectly. But remember: if a student finds you through Udemy’s search or ads, you only earn 3% of the sale (often a few cents per course).

Teachable’s Built-in Reach (Limited)

Teachable does not have a centralized marketplace. Your students must come from your own marketing efforts—social media, email lists, blog posts, YouTube, or paid ads. This means you need to build your own audience or invest in advertising. The upside? You keep nearly 100% of your revenue (after fees), and you own the student relationship entirely.

Audience Reach Verdict

If you’re starting from scratch with no audience, Udemy can give you initial visibility and validation. But if you already have a following or are willing to build one, Teachable allows you to capture the full value of your traffic.

4. Monetization Options: How You Make Money

Teachable Monetization

Teachable gives you multiple ways to maximize revenue:

  • One-time courses: Sell courses at any price (e.g., $50–$500+).
  • Subscriptions: Offer monthly or yearly memberships for access to your entire course library.
  • Course bundles: Group courses together at a discount to increase average order value.
  • Payment plans: Let students pay in installments (e.g., 4 payments of $25).
  • Affiliate marketing: Recruit affiliates to promote your courses for a commission (built-in tool on Pro plan).
  • Upsells and cross-sells: Offer additional products (coaching, eBooks, etc.) within your school.

With Teachable, you can also sell courses through third-party platforms like Affiliately or PartnerStack for advanced affiliate management.

Udemy Monetization

Udemy’s monetization is simpler but more restrictive:

  • One-time course sales: Udemy sets the price (usually $12.99–$199.99, but heavily discounted).
  • Instructor coupons: You can generate coupons to offer discounts or give courses away for free (to build reviews).
  • Udemy for Business: Your course may be included in Udemy’s corporate subscription, earning you a share of subscription revenue based on watch time.

You cannot sell subscriptions, bundles, or upsells directly on Udemy. All transactions happen through Udemy’s checkout, and you have no direct access to student email addresses (unless they opt in via your external links).

Monetization Verdict

Teachable is the clear winner for monetization flexibility. You can create a full-fledged digital product ecosystem. Udemy is best for passive income from a high-volume, low-price model—but your earnings per student are tiny.
