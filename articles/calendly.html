Calendly vs Cal.com: The Ultimate Showdown for Scheduling in 2024

In the fast-paced world of modern business, scheduling tools have become indispensable. Whether you're a freelancer juggling client calls, a sales team booking demos, or a enterprise managing complex workflows, the right scheduling software can save you hours each week. Two names dominate this space: the established giant Calendly and the rising open-source contender Cal.com. This detailed comparison will help you decide which platform best fits your needs, budget, and technical requirements.

We'll break down features, pricing, customization, user experience, and scalability—all while keeping the focus on how each tool solves real-world scheduling pain points.

What is Calendly?

Calendly, launched in 2013, is the most widely recognized scheduling automation platform. It pioneered the concept of sharing a simple link that lets invitees choose from your available time slots, eliminating the dreaded "What time works for you?" email chain. Calendly integrates with over 1,000 apps including Google Calendar, Outlook, Zoom, Salesforce, and HubSpot. It’s known for its polished user interface, reliability, and enterprise-grade features.

What is Cal.com?

Cal.com (formerly Calendso) is an open-source alternative that launched in 2021. It offers all core scheduling functionality—availability management, multiple event types, team scheduling, and integrations—but with a key difference: you can self-host the software on your own servers. This appeals to privacy-conscious users, developers, and organizations that want full control over their data. Cal.com also offers a cloud-hosted SaaS version for those who prefer a managed experience.

Detailed Feature Comparison

1. Core Scheduling Capabilities

Calendly offers a mature, battle-tested scheduling engine. You can create multiple event types (15-minute phone calls, 30-minute webinars, 60-minute consultations), set buffer times between meetings, define availability windows, and add custom questions to booking forms. It handles time zones flawlessly and sends automated email and SMS reminders.

Cal.com matches every core feature: multiple event types, custom durations, buffer times, availability schedules, and booking questions. It also supports round-robin and collective scheduling for teams. While Cal.com’s feature set is nearly identical, its interface feels slightly less polished—though it's improving rapidly with each monthly release.

2. Integrations and Ecosystem

Calendly boasts the largest integration library. Native connections to Google Calendar, Office 365, iCloud, Zoom, Google Meet, Microsoft Teams, Salesforce, HubSpot, Slack, Stripe, PayPal, Zapier, and hundreds more. For enterprise users, Calendly offers advanced Salesforce and Marketo integrations for lead routing and attribution.

Cal.com covers the essentials: Google Calendar, Outlook, Zoom, Google Meet, and Microsoft Teams. It integrates with Zapier for thousands of additional apps. However, it lacks some premium native integrations (like deep Salesforce or HubSpot sync) that Calendly offers. The open-source community contributes new integrations regularly, but the ecosystem is smaller.

3. Pricing and Plans

Calendly uses a freemium model with three paid tiers:

  • Free: One event type, one calendar connection, basic integrations
  • Essentials ($10/month): Unlimited event types, multiple calendars, custom reminders, and payment collection
  • Professional ($16/month): Team scheduling, round-robin, workflows, and advanced integrations
  • Teams ($20/month per user): Team analytics, Salesforce integration, and priority support
  • Enterprise (custom): SSO, audit logs, admin controls, and dedicated support

Cal.com offers a generous free tier and affordable paid plans:

  • Free (Cloud): Unlimited event types, one calendar, basic integrations, 1 user
  • Essentials ($12/month): Unlimited users, multiple calendars, workflows, and team scheduling
  • Teams ($20/month per user): Advanced workflows, custom branding, and priority support
  • Enterprise (custom): Self-hosting, SSO, audit logs, and white-label options

Notably, Cal.com’s free tier is more generous (unlimited event types vs. Calendly’s one), and its Essentials plan includes unlimited users—a major advantage for small teams.

4. Customization and Branding

Calendly offers limited customization on lower plans. You can add a logo and change colors on Professional and above. Custom domain and full white-labeling require an Enterprise plan. The booking page is clean and professional, but you can't deeply modify its layout or CSS.

Cal.com provides more flexibility. On cloud plans, you can customize colors, fonts, and add custom CSS. Self-hosted users have complete control over the booking page appearance, including full white-labeling. This makes Cal.com ideal for agencies or consultants who want to present a fully branded experience without paying enterprise premiums.

5. Self-Hosting and Privacy

This is where the two platforms diverge most dramatically. Calendly is 100% cloud-hosted. All your data resides on Calendly’s servers, processed under their privacy policy. For organizations with strict data residency requirements (healthcare, finance, government), this can be a dealbreaker.

Cal.com is open-source and can be self-hosted on your own infrastructure (Docker, Kubernetes, or a simple VPS). You control all data, encryption keys, and backups. This is a massive advantage for GDPR compliance, HIPAA considerations, or any scenario where data sovereignty matters. The open-source nature also means you can audit the code for security vulnerabilities.

6. User Experience and Interface

Calendly excels here. Its interface is intuitive, polished, and requires zero technical skill. Setting up your first booking link takes under two minutes. The dashboard is clean, with clear navigation for event types, availability, and integrations. For non-technical users, Calendly is the gold standard.

Cal.com has a steeper learning curve, especially for self-hosted users. The cloud version is user-friendly, but some advanced settings (like workflows or routing forms) require more clicks and configuration. The open-source version demands comfort with command-line tools and server management. However, the community provides excellent documentation and active support forums.

Practical Tips and Actionable Advice

When to Choose Calendly

  • You need a plug-and-play solution: If you have no technical team and want scheduling working in minutes, Calendly is the safer bet.
  • You rely on Salesforce or HubSpot: Calendly’s deep CRM integrations are unmatched for sales workflows.
  • You prioritize a polished experience: Calendly’s booking pages look professional out-of-the-box, which matters for client-facing businesses.
  • You need extensive app integrations: With 1,000+ native connections, Calendly is more likely to work with your existing tech stack.

When to Choose Cal.com

  • Data privacy is critical: Self-hosting gives you complete control. This is non-negotiable for healthcare, legal, or finance professionals.
  • You have technical resources: If you or your team can manage a server, self-hosting saves money and offers unlimited customization.
  • You want unlimited users on a budget: Cal.com’s $12/month Essentials plan covers unlimited team members—a huge saving compared to Calendly’s per-user pricing.
  • You need white-labeling without enterprise pricing: Self-hosted Cal.com allows full branding control at no extra cost.

Hybrid Approach

Some organizations use both: Calendly for external client-facing scheduling (where polish and integrations matter) and self-hosted Cal.com for internal team scheduling or sensitive meetings. This gives you the best of both worlds, though it adds complexity.
