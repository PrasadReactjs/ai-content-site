What is Health Tech and Biohacking?

Before we jump into the gadgets, let’s define our terms. Health tech refers to the use of technology—apps, wearables, sensors, and telemedicine—to monitor and improve health outcomes. Biohacking is the proactive, often DIY approach to manipulating your biology. Think of it as the intersection of quantified self and performance enhancement.

Together, these fields are democratizing access to data that was once reserved for elite athletes or clinical researchers. Today, anyone with a smart ring and a continuous glucose monitor can fine-tune their sleep, diet, and cognition.

The Core Pillars of Modern Biohacking

  • Data Collection: Wearables that track heart rate variability (HRV), sleep stages, and blood oxygen.
  • Intervention: Nootropics, red light therapy, cold exposure, and intermittent fasting.
  • Feedback Loops: Using data to adjust lifestyle in real-time.

Top Health Tech Devices You Should Know About

The market is flooded with gadgets, but a few stand out for their accuracy and actionable insights. Here are the tools currently dominating the health tech space.

1. Continuous Glucose Monitors (CGMs) for Non-Diabetics

Once reserved for diabetics, CGMs like the Dexcom G7 and Abbott Freestyle Libre 3 are now being adopted by biohackers. Why? Because glucose spikes affect energy, mood, and long-term metabolic health. Wearing a CGM for two weeks reveals exactly how your body responds to oatmeal vs. eggs. The actionable tip? Eat fiber and protein before carbohydrates to flatten the glucose curve.

2. The Oura Ring Gen 4

This sleek ring tracks sleep latency, deep sleep duration, and HRV. The latest model adds daytime stress detection. Biohackers use it to answer: “Did my evening meditation actually improve my recovery score?” The data is clear—consistent sleep timing is the single most impactful biohack.

3. Smart Beds and Temperature Regulation

Devices like the Eight Sleep Pod Cover actively cool or heat your bed to optimize deep sleep. Studies show that a 1°C drop in core body temperature accelerates sleep onset. If you wake up groggy, your sleep environment might be the culprit.

Biohacking Protocols: What the Science Says

Gadgets are useless without protocols. Here are three evidence-based biohacks that deliver measurable results.

Cold Exposure for Dopamine

Dr. Andrew Huberman’s research popularized cold plunges. A 2023 study in Nature confirmed that brief cold exposure (11°C for 2 minutes) increases dopamine by 250% and sustains elevated mood for hours. Actionable tip: Start your shower cold for 30 seconds, then work up to 2 minutes. Do not do this if you have cardiovascular issues without consulting a doctor.

Red Light Therapy for Cellular Repair

Red and near-infrared light (630-850nm) penetrates the skin to boost mitochondrial function. A 2024 meta-analysis found that 10 minutes of red light therapy daily improved muscle recovery by 30% and reduced fine lines. Devices like the Joovv Mini make this accessible for home use.

Time-Restricted Eating (16:8 Protocol)

This is the most accessible biohack. By limiting your eating window to 8 hours, you increase autophagy (cellular cleanup) and improve insulin sensitivity. Pair this with a CGM to see how your glucose stabilizes after 3 days of consistent timing.

Practical Tips: How to Start Biohacking Today

You don’t need a $2,000 smart bed to begin. Start with these low-cost, high-impact steps.

  • Measure your sleep environment: Use a free app like Sleep Cycle to detect snoring or restless periods.
  • Test your baseline: Check your HRV using a $50 chest strap (like the Polar H10) every morning for a week.
  • One supplement at a time: Try magnesium glycinate (200mg) before bed for deeper sleep. Track the effect in a journal.
  • Reduce blue light: Wear blue-blocking glasses 2 hours before bed. This is cheap and proven to increase melatonin production.

The Ethical Side of Biohacking

As with any frontier, there are risks. The health tech and biohacking community emphasizes “first, do no harm.” Avoid unregulated supplements (especially those sold on obscure websites). Be skeptical of influencers selling “miracle” stacks. The best biohacks are boring: sleep, nutrition, movement, and stress management.

Also, remember that data can cause anxiety. If tracking your HRV makes you obsess over numbers, step back. Biohacking should empower you, not enslave you.
