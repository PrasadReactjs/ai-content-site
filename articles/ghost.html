Ghost vs Substack: The Ultimate Showdown for Creators in 2024

If you're a writer, podcaster, or content creator looking to monetize your audience, you've likely encountered two major platforms: Ghost and Substack. While both offer powerful tools for publishing newsletters and building subscription-based businesses, they cater to very different needs. This comprehensive comparison will help you decide which platform aligns with your goals, technical skills, and long-term vision.

What Are Ghost and Substack?

Ghost is an open-source, self-hosted content management system (CMS) designed for professional publishing. It offers full control over your website, design, and data. Think of it as a premium alternative to WordPress, specifically optimized for subscriptions and memberships.

Substack is a hosted, all-in-one platform that simplifies newsletter creation and monetization. It handles everything from email delivery to payment processing, making it incredibly easy to start a paid newsletter in minutes.

Detailed Feature Comparison

1. Ease of Use

Substack wins for absolute beginners. You sign up, import your email list, and start writing. There's no setup, no hosting, and no technical decisions. The editor is clean and distraction-free, reminiscent of Medium.

Ghost has a steeper learning curve. If you self-host, you'll need to manage servers, domains, and SSL certificates. Even with Ghost(Pro), you'll spend time configuring themes, memberships, and integrations. However, this complexity unlocks far more power.

2. Customization and Design

This is where Ghost truly shines. You have complete control over your site's appearance through themes (both free and premium). You can modify every pixel using HTML, CSS, and even JavaScript. Ghost supports custom pages, navigation menus, and advanced SEO settings.

Substack offers only a handful of templates with limited customization. You can change your logo, colors, and font, but you're essentially locked into Substack's brand aesthetic. Your newsletter will look like every other Substack publication.

3. Monetization Features

Both platforms support paid subscriptions, but Ghost goes further. With Ghost, you can:

  • Offer multiple membership tiers (e.g., free, $5/month, $10/month)
  • Create one-time digital products or premium content
  • Accept tips and donations
  • Integrate with Stripe for flexible payment options
  • Set up recurring billing with custom trial periods

Substack keeps it simple: one paid tier (you set the price) and free content. Substack takes a 10% cut of all subscription revenue, plus Stripe processing fees. Ghost(Pro) charges a flat platform fee (starting at $9/month) with no revenue share.

4. Email Deliverability and Analytics

Both platforms handle email delivery, but Ghost gives you more control. Ghost allows you to connect your own email service (like Mailgun or Amazon SES) for better deliverability and lower costs at scale. You also get detailed analytics on open rates, click-through rates, and subscriber growth.

Substack manages email delivery internally, which works well for most creators. However, you have limited control over deliverability issues, and analytics are more basic (open rates, subscriber counts, and revenue).

5. SEO and Content Ownership

Ghost is built for SEO. It generates clean, semantic HTML, supports meta descriptions, and allows custom URLs. Your content lives on your own domain, building your site's authority over time. You own 100% of your content and subscriber data, and you can export everything at any time.

Substack provides basic SEO but your content lives on substack.com/yournewsletter. You don't own your subscriber list in the same way—Substack retains rights to use your content for platform promotion. Exporting your data is possible but not as seamless as Ghost.

Pricing Breakdown

Ghost Pricing

  • Self-hosted: Free (you pay for server hosting, typically $5-20/month)
  • Ghost(Pro): Starts at $9/month for up to 500 members, $25/month for 1,000 members, scaling up to enterprise plans
  • No transaction fees on subscriptions

Substack Pricing

  • Free to start (no monthly fee)
  • 10% transaction fee on all paid subscriptions
  • Stripe processing fees (2.9% + $0.30 per transaction)

For a creator earning $1,000/month from subscriptions: Ghost(Pro) costs $9/month total. Substack costs $100 in transaction fees + processing fees. Over a year, that's $108 vs over $1,200—a significant difference.

When to Choose Ghost

Choose Ghost if you:

  • Want full control over your brand and website design
  • Plan to scale beyond a simple newsletter (courses, memberships, products)
  • Care deeply about SEO and owning your domain authority
  • Prefer predictable monthly costs over revenue-sharing
  • Have some technical ability or budget to hire a developer
  • Want to build a long-term, multi-faceted content business

When to Choose Substack

Choose Substack if you:

  • Want to start publishing immediately with zero setup
  • Have limited technical skills and no budget for development
  • Focus purely on writing newsletters (not building a full website)
  • Prefer a simple, all-in-one solution without managing integrations
  • Don't mind the 10% revenue share and platform lock-in
  • Are testing the waters and may pivot later

Practical Tips for Making the Switch

If you're currently on Substack but considering Ghost, here's actionable advice:

  • Export your content: Substack allows you to export all posts as HTML or Markdown. Ghost can import these directly.
  • Export your subscriber list: Download your subscriber CSV from Substack. Note: email subscribers who joined via Substack's recommendation system may not export.
  • Set up a staging environment: Before going live, test Ghost on a subdomain (e.g., test.yourdomain.com) to ensure everything works.
  • Redirect your old links: Use Ghost's built-in redirects to point old Substack URLs to your new Ghost site. This preserves SEO value.
  • Notify your audience: Send a final email on Substack explaining the move and why it benefits them (better reading experience, more features).

Conclusion: Which Platform Should You Choose?

The answer depends entirely on your goals and resources. Ghost is the superior choice for serious creators who want to build a sustainable, independent media business. It offers more flexibility, better economics at scale, and true ownership of your work. The initial effort to set up Ghost pays off exponentially as you grow.
