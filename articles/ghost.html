Ghost vs Substack: The Ultimate Comparison for Creators in 2024

If you're a writer, blogger, or creator looking to monetize your content, you've likely encountered two major platforms: Ghost and Substack. Both offer powerful tools for publishing newsletters and building subscription-based revenue, but they cater to very different needs. In this detailed comparison, we’ll break down every aspect—from pricing and customization to ownership and scalability—so you can decide which platform aligns with your goals. Our target keyword is "Ghost," and we’ll explore how it stacks up against Substack in this comparison.

What Are Ghost and Substack?

Ghost is an open-source content management system (CMS) designed for modern publishing. It’s a fully self-hosted or managed platform that gives you complete control over your website, design, and data. Ghost is ideal for creators who want a professional, customizable site with built-in membership and subscription features.

Substack, on the other hand, is a hosted platform focused exclusively on newsletters. It’s a turnkey solution where you can start writing and charging subscribers in minutes. Substack handles everything—hosting, payments, and email delivery—but you trade control for convenience.

This Ghost vs Substack comparison will help you understand the trade-offs.

Detailed Feature Comparison

1. Ease of Use

Substack wins on simplicity. You sign up, write an email, and hit publish. The interface is minimalist, and there’s no technical setup. It’s perfect for writers who want to focus solely on content without touching code or design.

Ghost has a steeper learning curve. Self-hosting requires technical know-how (server setup, domain configuration), but Ghost Pro (managed hosting) simplifies this. The editor is clean and powerful, but you’ll need to customize themes or hire a developer for a unique look. However, once set up, Ghost is intuitive for daily publishing.

2. Customization and Branding

This is where Ghost shines. You have full control over every pixel—custom themes, CSS, JavaScript, and even API integrations. You can build a membership site, add a podcast, or create a course. Ghost is a true CMS, not just a newsletter tool.

Substack offers very limited customization. You can change colors, add a logo, and tweak fonts, but your site will look like every other Substack. There’s no way to add custom pages, navigation menus, or advanced features. This is a major limitation for creators who want a unique brand.

3. Monetization and Fees

Substack takes a 10% cut of all subscription revenue. While there’s no upfront cost, this fee adds up. If you earn $10,000/month, Substack keeps $1,000. You also pay Stripe processing fees (2.9% + $0.30 per transaction). Substack only supports paid subscriptions—no one-time payments or donations.

Ghost has no revenue share. If you self-host, your only costs are hosting and email delivery (e.g., Mailgun). Ghost Pro plans start at $9/month and include hosting, email, and support. Ghost supports subscriptions, one-time tips, and even tiered memberships. You keep nearly 100% of your revenue after processing fees.

4. Ownership and Portability

With Ghost, you own everything—your content, subscriber list, and data. You can export your audience and move to another platform anytime. This is critical for long-term creators who don’t want to be locked in.

Substack allows you to export your subscriber emails, but the platform’s terms have been criticized. Substack can terminate accounts, and you lose your built-in audience if you leave. Your content lives on Substack’s servers, and you have limited control over your site’s future.

5. Email Delivery and Analytics

Both platforms offer built-in email delivery. Ghost uses Mailgun (or your own SMTP provider) for high deliverability. You get detailed analytics on open rates, clicks, and subscriber growth. Ghost also supports drip campaigns and automated emails.

Substack handles email delivery automatically, but analytics are basic—you see open rates and subscriber counts. There’s no advanced segmentation or automation. For most writers, this is enough, but power users may find it limiting.

6. Scalability and Performance

Ghost is built for performance. It’s lightweight, fast, and can handle high traffic with proper hosting. Ghost Pro scales automatically, and you can add CDN, caching, and custom optimizations. It’s used by large publishers like OpenAI and DigitalOcean.

Substack is reliable for small to medium audiences, but you’re at the mercy of Substack’s infrastructure. If your newsletter grows rapidly, you may face delays or limitations. Substack also doesn’t offer advanced SEO or performance tools.

Practical Tips: Which Platform Should You Choose?

Choose Ghost if:

  • You want full control over your brand and design.
  • You plan to build a membership site, course, or media company.
  • You dislike revenue sharing and want to keep 100% of your earnings.
  • You value data ownership and portability.
  • You have technical skills or budget for a managed plan.

Choose Substack if:

  • You want the fastest, simplest way to start a paid newsletter.
  • You’re a solo writer who doesn’t care about customization.
  • You’re okay with paying 10% of revenue for convenience.
  • You don’t plan to scale beyond a newsletter.
  • You’re testing an idea and want zero upfront cost.

Actionable Advice for Migrating or Starting

If you’re already on Substack and considering Ghost, here’s a step-by-step plan:

  1. Export your subscriber list from Substack (Settings > Export).
  2. Choose Ghost Pro (starts at $9/month) to avoid technical hassle.
  3. Import your subscribers into Ghost (CSV import is supported).
  4. Redirect your Substack URL to your new Ghost site (use a 301 redirect).
  5. Notify your audience about the move via a final email on Substack.

For new creators: Start with Ghost if you have a clear vision for a brand. Start with Substack if you want to test a newsletter idea quickly with zero risk.
