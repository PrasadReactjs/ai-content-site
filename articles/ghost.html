Ghost vs Substack: The Ultimate Showdown for Creators in 2025

If you’re a writer, podcaster, or digital creator looking to monetize your content, you’ve likely encountered two major platforms: Ghost and Substack. Both promise to help you build an audience, publish newsletters, and earn revenue through subscriptions. But beneath the surface, they are fundamentally different tools. In this comprehensive comparison, we’ll break down every aspect—from pricing and customization to ownership and scalability—so you can decide which platform aligns with your long-term goals. Let’s dive in.

What Are Ghost and Substack?

Before we compare, let’s define each platform.

Ghost is an open-source, fully customizable content management system (CMS). Originally built for blogging, it has evolved into a powerful membership and newsletter platform. You can self-host it on your own server or use Ghost(Pro) for managed hosting. Ghost gives you complete control over your site’s design, data, and monetization models.

Substack is a hosted, all-in-one platform designed specifically for newsletter writers. It handles everything from email delivery to payment processing. Substack takes a cut of your subscription revenue (currently 10%) but offers a simple, “set it and forget it” experience. You don’t need to worry about hosting, security, or technical maintenance.

Now, let’s examine the key differences.

Pricing: Which Platform Saves You More Money?

Ghost Pricing

Ghost offers two pricing paths:

  • Ghost(Pro) Managed Hosting: Starts at $9/month for basic features and scales up to $199/month for high-traffic sites. There is no transaction fee on subscriptions—you keep 100% of your revenue after the hosting cost.
  • Self-Hosted Ghost: The software is free and open-source. You only pay for server hosting (e.g., DigitalOcean, AWS, or Linode), which can be as low as $5–$10/month. You also need to manage updates, security, and backups yourself.

Substack Pricing

Substack is free to start. You pay nothing to create an account, publish posts, or send emails. However, Substack takes a 10% cut of all subscription revenue. For example, if you earn $1,000/month in subscriptions, Substack keeps $100. Additionally, Stripe payment processing fees (2.9% + $0.30 per transaction) apply on top of that.

Winner for budget-conscious creators: Ghost, especially if you self-host or use Ghost(Pro) at scale. Over time, the 10% Substack fee can become substantial. For instance, a creator earning $5,000/month would pay $500/month to Substack, whereas Ghost(Pro) at $49/month would save you $451/month.

Customization and Design

Ghost: Total Creative Freedom

Ghost offers a rich, customizable front-end. You can choose from dozens of free and premium themes, or build your own using Handlebars.js templates. You can add custom pages, integrate third-party tools, and modify every element of your site’s look and feel. Ghost also supports multiple content types: blog posts, newsletters, podcasts, courses, and even members-only areas.

Substack: Simplicity Over Flexibility

Substack provides a handful of pre-designed templates. You can change your logo, colors, and font, but that’s about it. The layout is fixed—a linear, chronological feed of posts. You cannot create a complex website with landing pages, custom navigation, or advanced design. Substack prioritizes a uniform, distraction-free reading experience over creative control.

Winner for design control: Ghost. If you want your brand to stand out, Ghost is the clear choice.

Monetization Models

Ghost: Multi-Faceted Revenue Streams

Ghost allows you to monetize in several ways:

  • Paid subscriptions: Monthly or yearly tiers.
  • One-time donations or tips.
  • Paid content gating: Lock individual posts or entire sections.
  • Membership tiers: Offer different levels of access (e.g., free, premium, VIP).
  • Custom integrations: Sell courses, digital products, or physical goods via plugins.

Substack: Subscription-Only Model

Substack is built around recurring subscriptions. You can set a monthly or yearly price, and offer free or paid posts. You can also run a “founding member” tier at a higher price. However, Substack does not support one-time payments, donations, or product sales natively. You cannot gate specific content behind multiple tiers—only a single paid tier is available.

Winner for monetization flexibility: Ghost. Substack’s model is simpler but far more limited.

Ownership and Portability

Ghost: You Own Everything

Because Ghost is open-source and self-hostable, you have complete ownership of your content, subscriber list, and data. You can export your data at any time and migrate to another platform. There is no vendor lock-in. Your site lives on your domain, and you control SEO, analytics, and backups.

Substack: You’re a Tenant, Not an Owner

Substack hosts your content on its own domain (yoursubstack.com). While you can use a custom domain, you still rely on Substack’s infrastructure. If Substack changes its terms, shuts down, or bans your account, you could lose everything. Exporting your subscriber list is possible, but Substack has been criticized for making the process cumbersome. Also, Substack owns the relationship with your readers—they control email delivery and can modify your site’s appearance.

Winner for long-term security: Ghost. If you care about digital sovereignty, Ghost is the only choice.

Email Deliverability and Audience Growth

Ghost: Third-Party Email Integration

Ghost does not have its own email sending infrastructure. Instead, it integrates with services like Mailgun, Amazon SES, or Postmark. This gives you flexibility but requires a bit of technical setup. Deliverability depends on your email service provider’s reputation. Ghost also lacks built-in audience growth tools like recommendation engines or cross-promotion networks.

Substack: Built-In Email & Discovery

Substack handles all email delivery internally. It has a strong reputation for deliverability because it controls the infrastructure. Additionally, Substack has a powerful recommendation network: when readers subscribe to your newsletter, Substack suggests other popular publications. This can accelerate organic growth, especially for new writers.

Winner for ease of email and discovery: Substack. The built-in network effect is a significant advantage for growing an audience quickly.

SEO and Content Marketing

Ghost: SEO Powerhouse

Ghost offers full control over SEO. You can edit meta titles, descriptions, URLs, and structured data. It generates clean, fast-loading HTML and supports AMP (Accelerated Mobile Pages). Ghost also integrates with Google Analytics, Search Console, and other SEO tools. This makes it ideal for creators who want to drive traffic from search engines.

Substack: SEO Limitations

Substack’s SEO capabilities are basic. You can set a custom domain and edit post titles, but you have limited control over meta tags, canonical URLs, and site structure. Substack pages often load slower due to shared hosting. While some Substack publications rank well, it’s harder to compete in competitive niches.

Winner for search engine traffic: Ghost. If you plan to rely on organic search, Ghost is vastly superior.

Community and Support

Ghost: Documentation and Community

Ghost has excellent documentation and an active community forum. However, there is no official phone or live chat support unless you are on a higher Ghost(Pro) plan. Self-hosted users rely on community help and third-party tutorials.

Substack: Customer-Centric Support

Substack offers email support and a comprehensive help center. Because it’s a simpler platform, there are fewer things to troubleshoot. The support team is generally responsive and helpful for common issues.

Winner for beginners: Substack. The hand-holding experience is better for non-technical users.
