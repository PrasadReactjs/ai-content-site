Ghost vs Substack: The Ultimate Comparison for Creators in 2024

Choosing the right platform for your newsletter or content business is one of the most critical decisions you'll make as a creator. Two of the most popular options today are Ghost and Substack. While both allow you to publish articles, grow an email list, and monetize your work, they approach the problem from very different angles. In this detailed comparison, we’ll break down everything you need to know—pricing, features, customization, ownership, and scalability—so you can decide which platform is right for you.

What Is Ghost?

Ghost is an open-source, headless CMS (content management system) designed specifically for modern publishing. It was originally created as a lightweight alternative to WordPress, but has since evolved into a powerful platform for newsletters, membership sites, and paid subscriptions. Ghost gives you full control over your content, design, and data. You can self-host it (using your own server or a service like DigitalOcean) or use Ghost(Pro), the company’s managed hosting solution.

What Is Substack?

Substack is a hosted platform that lets writers start a newsletter and monetize it with paid subscriptions in minutes. It’s famously simple: you sign up, write, and publish. Substack handles hosting, email delivery, and payment processing for you. In exchange, it takes a 10% cut of your subscription revenue. Substack is often described as the "Twitter for newsletters" because of its low barrier to entry and built-in social features.

Key Differences at a Glance

  • Ownership: Ghost is open-source and self-hostable; Substack is a closed, proprietary platform.
  • Pricing: Ghost has a fixed monthly fee (or free if you self-host); Substack is free to start but takes 10% of your revenue.
  • Customization: Ghost offers full control over design, themes, and code; Substack has very limited customization.
  • Features: Ghost includes built-in membership management, SEO tools, and API access; Substack focuses on simplicity and discovery.

Detailed Comparison: Ghost vs Substack

1. Pricing and Revenue Share

Ghost has a clear, predictable pricing model. Ghost(Pro) plans start at $9/month for up to 500 members, $25/month for up to 1,000 members, and scale up to $299/month for 100,000 members. There are no transaction fees—you keep 100% of your subscription revenue (minus payment processor fees, typically 2-3%). If you’re technical, you can self-host Ghost for free, paying only for server costs (as low as $5-10/month).

Substack is free to create an account and start publishing. However, Substack takes a 10% cut of all paid subscriptions. For example, if you charge $10/month per subscriber and have 1,000 paying subscribers, you earn $9,000/month—but Substack takes $1,000. Stripe processing fees (2.9% + $0.30 per transaction) also apply. Over time, this revenue share can become very expensive.

Verdict: If you have a large or growing subscriber base, Ghost is far more cost-effective. For small, early-stage newsletters, Substack’s zero upfront cost is attractive.

2. Customization and Design

Ghost offers a wide range of free and premium themes, and you can fully customize them using HTML, CSS, and Handlebars templates. You can also build a complete website with navigation, landing pages, tags, and custom post types. Ghost supports code injection, custom integrations, and a powerful API. This makes it ideal for creators who want a unique brand and a professional website.

Substack has a very minimal, standardized design. You can add a logo, change colors, and set a custom domain, but you cannot change the layout or add custom code. All Substack newsletters look similar. This is intentional—Substack prioritizes simplicity over flexibility.

Verdict: Ghost is the clear winner for creators who value branding and design. Substack is for those who want to start writing immediately without worrying about aesthetics.

3. Email and Deliverability

Both platforms handle email delivery, but they do it differently. Ghost uses Amazon Simple Email Service (SES) by default, which is reliable and cost-effective. You can also integrate with third-party email services like Mailgun, SendGrid, or Postmark for advanced deliverability. Ghost gives you full control over your email list, including export options and segmentation.

Substack uses its own in-house email infrastructure. Deliverability is generally good, but you have limited control. You cannot easily export your email list (though you can request it). Substack also adds its own branding to your emails (e.g., "Substack" in the footer), which some creators dislike.

Verdict: Ghost offers more control and better deliverability options, especially for large lists. Substack is simpler but less flexible.

4. Monetization and Memberships

Ghost has a native membership system that supports free, paid, and premium tiers. You can set up recurring subscriptions (monthly or yearly), offer multiple pricing plans, and create member-only content. Ghost also supports one-time donations and can integrate with Stripe or PayPal. You have full control over pricing, discounts, and trial periods.

Substack also supports free and paid subscriptions, but its monetization is more limited. You can only have one paid tier per publication. Substack’s "recommendations" feature can help you grow, but it also promotes other Substack writers, which may send your subscribers away.

Verdict: Ghost is more flexible and offers more monetization options. Substack is simpler but less powerful for advanced membership models.

5. SEO and Content Discovery

Ghost is built with SEO in mind. It generates clean, semantic HTML, supports meta tags, sitemaps, canonical URLs, and structured data. You can fully optimize each post for search engines. Ghost also has a built-in AMP (Accelerated Mobile Pages) feature for faster loading on mobile devices.

Substack has limited SEO capabilities. You can set a custom domain and write meta descriptions, but Substack adds its own URL structure and page elements that can hurt your rankings. Substack publications also compete with each other in search results.

Verdict: Ghost is far superior for SEO and organic traffic. If you want to build a long-term content business with search engine traffic, choose Ghost.

6. Data Ownership and Portability

Ghost is open-source, meaning you own all your content, data, and subscriber information. You can export everything at any time—posts, members, email analytics, and settings. If you self-host, you have complete control over your server and database.

Substack owns your content and subscriber list. While you can export your posts as a CSV file, you cannot easily export your email list. If Substack changes its terms, pricing, or features, you have little recourse. Moving away from Substack is difficult and time-consuming.

Verdict: Ghost gives you full ownership and portability. Substack locks you into its ecosystem.

Practical Tips for Choosing Between Ghost and Substack

  • Start with Substack if: You are a solo writer testing the waters, have no technical skills, and want to start publishing in minutes. Substack’s low barrier to entry is perfect for beginners.
  • Choose Ghost if: You are serious about building a long-term content business, want full ownership, need advanced customization, or plan to scale beyond a few hundred subscribers.
  • Consider a hybrid approach: Some creators start on Substack to validate their idea, then migrate to Ghost when they outgrow Substack’s limitations. However, migration is not seamless—you’ll lose your Substack subscriber list.
  • Test both: Sign up for a free trial of Ghost(Pro) (14 days) and create a free Substack account. Publish a few posts on each to see which workflow you prefer.
