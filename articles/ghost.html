Ghost vs Substack: Which Platform is Right for Your Newsletter in 2024?

If you're looking to start a newsletter or a content-driven website, you've likely come across two major players: Ghost and Substack. Both platforms allow you to publish content, build an audience, and monetize your work through subscriptions. However, they take fundamentally different approaches. This detailed comparison will help you decide which platform aligns with your goals, technical skills, and long-term vision.

Ghost is an open-source, fully customizable content management system (CMS) designed for creators, publishers, and businesses. Substack, on the other hand, is a hosted, all-in-one platform that prioritizes simplicity and ease of use. Let's dive into the key differences.

1. Ownership and Control

Ghost: Full Ownership

With Ghost, you own everything. Because it's open-source software (MIT license), you can self-host on your own server or use Ghost (Pro) as a managed service. Your content, subscriber list, and data are fully portable. You can export your data at any time without restrictions. This is crucial if you ever want to switch platforms or customize your site beyond what a hosted service allows.

Substack: Platform Dependency

Substack is a closed, hosted platform. While you own the intellectual property of your writing, your audience and subscriber data live on Substack's servers. You cannot easily export your subscriber list (you can export email addresses, but not engagement data or custom fields). If Substack changes its terms, pricing, or features, you have limited recourse. Many creators feel locked in because migrating an established audience is difficult.

Practical Tip: If long-term ownership and data portability matter to you, Ghost is the clear winner. If you're testing the waters and don't want to worry about technical setup, Substack's simplicity might be appealing.

2. Monetization and Pricing

Ghost: Flexible Monetization & Lower Fees

Ghost offers built-in membership and subscription features. You can set up paid subscriptions (monthly, yearly, or tiered plans) without using third-party services like Stripe or PayPal—though it supports both. Ghost takes 0% commission on subscriptions (you only pay payment processing fees, typically 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction). You can also sell digital products, accept one-time tips, or run a free newsletter with optional paid tiers.

  • Pricing: Self-hosting is free (you pay for server hosting, ~$10–$30/month). Ghost (Pro) managed hosting starts at $9/month for a basic plan.
  • Commission: 0% from Ghost.

Substack: Simple but Costly

Substack takes a 10% commission on all subscription revenue. For example, if you earn $1,000/month from subscribers, Substack takes $100. Plus, you pay Stripe processing fees (2.9% + $0.30) on top of that. Substack's simplicity is attractive—you just write and publish—but the fees add up significantly as you grow.

  • Pricing: Free to start (Substack covers hosting).
  • Commission: 10% of subscription revenue.

Actionable Advice: If you plan to earn a significant income from subscriptions, Ghost saves you thousands of dollars over time. For a small side project, Substack's 10% fee might be acceptable.

3. Customization and Design

Ghost: Unlimited Customization

Ghost is built for customization. You can choose from hundreds of themes (free and premium), edit HTML/CSS directly, or build a completely custom design. You have full control over your site's layout, navigation, typography, and branding. Ghost also supports custom integrations, APIs, and advanced SEO settings (meta descriptions, structured data, canonical URLs, etc.).

Substack: Limited Design Options

Substack offers a uniform, minimal design. You can customize your logo, header image, and a few colors, but you cannot change the layout, add custom CSS, or create a unique website experience. All Substack newsletters look very similar. This is intentional—Substack prioritizes simplicity over design flexibility.

Practical Tip: If you want a branded, professional website that stands out, Ghost is the only choice. If you're fine with a simple, text-focused newsletter, Substack's limitations may not bother you.

4. Features and Functionality

Ghost: All-in-One CMS

Ghost is a full-fledged CMS. Key features include:

  • Built-in membership management (free, paid, and tiered subscriptions)
  • Email newsletters (send from Ghost or integrate with Mailgun, SendGrid, etc.)
  • SEO tools (meta tags, sitemaps, AMP support)
  • Analytics (dashboard with subscriber growth, email open rates, etc.)
  • Content scheduling and post types (articles, pages, podcasts, videos)
  • API access for custom integrations
  • Multi-language support

Substack: Focused on Newsletters

Substack is designed primarily for email newsletters. Features include:

  • Simple editor (WYSIWYG, supports images and embeds)
  • Subscriber management (basic segmentation)
  • Paid subscriptions (with 10% commission)
  • Comments and community features (threaded discussions)
  • Podcast hosting (limited)
  • Basic analytics (subscriber count, email open rates)

Actionable Insight: If you need a website, blog, or advanced features like custom membership tiers or API integrations, Ghost is far more capable. Substack is best if you only want to send emails and don't need a website.

5. Ease of Use and Learning Curve

Ghost: Moderate Learning Curve

Ghost is intuitive once set up, but it requires some technical knowledge if you self-host. Even with Ghost (Pro), you'll need to configure domain, email delivery, and membership settings. The editor is clean and distraction-free, but you'll have to learn its markdown-based formatting (or use the rich text editor).

Substack: Extremely Simple

Substack is designed for non-technical users. You sign up, write a post, and hit publish. There's no setup, no hosting, no configuration. The learning curve is near zero. This is Substack's biggest advantage.

Tip for Beginners: If you're not comfortable with any technical setup, start with Substack. You can always migrate to Ghost later if you outgrow it. But be aware that migrating subscribers is difficult.

6. Community and Discovery

Ghost: No Built-In Discovery

Ghost does not have a built-in discovery network. You are responsible for driving traffic through SEO, social media, guest posting, or paid advertising. This gives you full control but requires marketing effort.

Substack: Network Effects

Substack has a "Network" feature where readers can discover newsletters via recommendations, cross-promotions, and a directory. This can help new writers gain subscribers without paid ads. However, the network effect is modest compared to platforms like Medium.

Actionable Advice: If you already have an audience or are willing to invest in marketing, Ghost gives you better long-term ROI. If you're starting from zero and want some free discovery, Substack's network might help.

7. SEO and Search Engine Visibility

Ghost: SEO Powerhouse

Ghost is built with SEO in mind. It generates clean, semantic HTML, supports custom meta tags, structured data (Schema.org), canonical URLs, and automatic sitemaps. You can also install SEO plugins or modify the code. This makes Ghost excellent for ranking in Google and driving organic traffic.

Substack: Weak SEO

Substack pages are often indexed by Google, but they lack advanced SEO controls. You cannot edit meta descriptions, set custom URLs, or optimize for specific keywords. Substack also adds its own branding to your pages, which can dilute your SEO authority.
