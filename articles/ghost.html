Ghost vs Substack: The Ultimate Showdown for Creators in 2024

If you're a writer, podcaster, or content creator looking to monetize your audience, you've likely encountered two major platforms: Ghost and Substack. Both promise a path to independence, but they approach the job very differently. This comprehensive comparison will help you decide which platform is the right fit for your goals. We’ll dive deep into features, pricing, ownership, and long-term strategy, with a special focus on why Ghost is increasingly the choice for serious creators who want full control.

What Are Ghost and Substack?

Ghost is an open-source, fully customizable content management system (CMS) designed for modern publishing. It started as a Kickstarter project in 2013 and has since evolved into a powerful platform for newsletters, membership sites, and paid subscriptions. You can host it yourself (self-hosted) or use Ghost's managed hosting service (Ghost(Pro)).

Substack, launched in 2017, is a hosted platform that lets writers start a paid newsletter in minutes. It handles everything—hosting, payments, and email delivery—in exchange for a cut of your revenue. Substack is famous for its simplicity and network effects.

Key Comparison: Ghost vs Substack

1. Ownership and Control

Ghost gives you complete ownership. Because it's open-source, you own your content, your subscriber list, and your data. You can export everything at any time and move to another host. With the self-hosted version, you control the server, the code, and the future of your publication. This is a massive advantage if you ever want to scale or pivot.

Substack, by contrast, is a closed platform. You publish on Substack's servers, and your subscribers are stored in Substack's database. While you can export your subscriber emails, you cannot easily take your paid subscribers or your content's structure with you. If Substack changes its terms, pricing, or policies, you have limited recourse.

2. Pricing and Revenue Share

This is where the financial math becomes critical.

  • Ghost (self-hosted): Free software. You pay for hosting (e.g., $10–$50/month on DigitalOcean or AWS) and Stripe payment processing fees (2.9% + $0.30 per transaction). No platform commission.
  • Ghost(Pro): Managed hosting starts at $9/month (for 500 members) and scales up. No revenue share beyond Stripe fees.
  • Substack: Free to start. Substack takes a 10% cut of all subscription revenue (plus Stripe fees). If you earn $100,000/year, Substack takes $10,000.

Practical tip: If you plan to earn more than a few thousand dollars a year, Ghost quickly becomes cheaper. For a creator making $50,000/year, Substack costs $5,000 in platform fees. Ghost (self-hosted) costs about $600/year for hosting. That's over $4,000 saved annually.

3. Features and Customization

Ghost is a full-fledged CMS with a rich editor, built-in SEO tools, member management, analytics, and a powerful API. You can create custom themes, integrate with any third-party tool (via Zapier or direct API), and build complex membership tiers. It supports multiple content types: newsletters, blog posts, podcasts, videos, and even digital products.

Substack is deliberately minimalist. Its editor is simple, and customization options are limited. You can add basic images, embed videos, and create a simple landing page. But you cannot change the design significantly, add custom code, or integrate deeply with other tools. Substack does offer a "Substack Chat" feature and podcast hosting, but it's a walled garden.

4. Email Delivery and Audience Building

Both platforms handle email delivery, but they differ in approach.

Ghost uses its own open-source email system (based on the Mailgun API) or integrates with Amazon SES, Mailgun, or SendGrid. You have full control over deliverability and can manage bounce rates and spam complaints. This is crucial for building a healthy list.

Substack uses its own email infrastructure. While it's generally reliable, you have no visibility into deliverability metrics. Substack also has a built-in "network effect": your subscribers can discover other Substack writers, which can help you grow—but it also means your audience is part of Substack's ecosystem, not exclusively yours.

5. SEO and Discoverability

Ghost is built for SEO. It generates clean, semantic HTML, supports custom meta titles and descriptions, and allows full control over URL structures. You can also use custom themes optimized for search engines. Combined with fast load times (thanks to Node.js), Ghost sites tend to rank well on Google.

Substack has basic SEO, but its pages are less customizable. You cannot change URL slugs easily, and the default design is not optimized for search. Substack relies more on internal discovery and social sharing than organic search traffic.

6. Community and Support

Ghost has a vibrant open-source community, extensive documentation, and a dedicated forum. Ghost(Pro) users get priority email support. The self-hosted version relies on community support, but the documentation is thorough.

Substack offers email support and a knowledge base, but there is no community forum. Because you cannot customize the platform, support is limited to basic troubleshooting.

When to Choose Ghost

  • You want full ownership of your content and subscriber list.
  • You plan to scale beyond a simple newsletter (e.g., courses, memberships, digital products).
  • You are comfortable with a bit of technical setup (or willing to pay for managed hosting).
  • You care about SEO and want to drive organic traffic.
  • You want to avoid paying a 10% revenue share long-term.

When to Choose Substack

  • You want to start publishing immediately with zero technical setup.
  • You prefer a minimalist, distraction-free writing environment.
  • You value Substack's built-in audience discovery features.
  • You are not concerned about platform lock-in or revenue share.
  • You only plan to write a simple paid newsletter (no complex site).

Practical Tips for Migrating or Starting

If You Choose Ghost:

  1. Start with Ghost(Pro) if you're not technical. It's affordable and you can always migrate to self-hosted later.
  2. Use a custom domain from day one. It builds brand equity and helps with SEO.
  3. Set up Stripe for payments. Ghost integrates natively with Stripe for subscriptions.
  4. Import your existing subscribers from Substack or other platforms using Ghost's import tool.
  5. Customize your theme to match your brand. Ghost has many free and premium themes.

If You Choose Substack (and might switch later):

  1. Regularly export your subscriber list (Settings > Export). Keep a backup.
  2. Use your own domain (custom domain) on Substack so you can redirect it later.
  3. Avoid deep reliance on Substack's exclusive features (like Chat) if you plan to move.
  4. Start building an email list outside Substack (e.g., using Mailchimp) as a safety net.

Conclusion: The Verdict

If you are a serious creator who values ownership, customization, and long-term cost savings, Ghost is the superior choice. It gives you the freedom to build a sustainable media business without surrendering control or paying perpetual commissions. The initial setup effort (or modest hosting fee) pays off massively as you grow.

Substack is a fantastic tool for getting started quickly, especially if you're testing an idea or writing a simple newsletter. But for anyone with ambitions of building a real business, the limitations and revenue share become problematic over time.
