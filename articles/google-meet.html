Zoom vs Google Meet: Which Video Conferencing Platform Wins in 2024?

In the modern workplace, video conferencing is no longer a luxury—it's a necessity. Two platforms dominate the conversation: Zoom and Google Meet. While both offer robust solutions for remote communication, they cater to different needs and workflows. This detailed comparison will help you decide which platform is right for your team, with a special focus on the Google Meet ecosystem and how it stacks up against its biggest competitor.

Whether you're a solopreneur, a small business owner, or part of a large enterprise, understanding the nuances of each platform—from pricing and features to security and integrations—is critical. Let's dive into a head-to-head comparison of Zoom vs Google Meet.

Quick Overview: Zoom vs Google Meet

Before we get into the weeds, here's a high-level snapshot:

  • Zoom is a standalone video-first platform known for its robust feature set, large meeting capacity, and strong third-party integrations. It's ideal for webinars, large events, and teams that need advanced controls.
  • Google Meet is deeply integrated into the Google Workspace ecosystem (formerly G Suite). It's designed for seamless collaboration within Google Calendar, Gmail, Google Drive, and Google Chat. It's best for teams already invested in Google's productivity tools.

Now, let's break down each category in detail.

Pricing and Plans

Google Meet Pricing

One of the strongest selling points for Google Meet is its generous free tier and affordable paid plans. The free version of Google Meet offers:

  • Up to 100 participants
  • 60-minute meeting limit (increased to 24 hours until March 31, 2025, then reverts to 60 minutes)
  • Screen sharing, captions, and background effects

Paid plans (Google Workspace) start at $6/user/month (Business Starter) and include:

  • Up to 150 participants (Business Starter)
  • Up to 500 participants (Business Plus and Enterprise)
  • Recording to Google Drive
  • No meeting time limits
  • Advanced security and admin controls

Zoom Pricing

Zoom's free tier is also competitive, offering:

  • Up to 100 participants
  • 40-minute meeting limit
  • Screen sharing and basic collaboration tools

Paid plans start at $15.99/user/month (Pro) and include:

  • Up to 100 participants (Pro)
  • Up to 300 participants (Business, $21.99/user/month)
  • Up to 500 participants (Business Plus)
  • No meeting time limits
  • Recording to cloud or local storage
  • Advanced features like breakout rooms, polling, and transcription

Key takeaway: For small teams and budget-conscious users, Google Meet offers a better free tier (60 minutes vs 40 minutes). Zoom's paid plans are more expensive but include more advanced features out of the box.

Core Features Comparison

Meeting Capacity and Duration

Both platforms support up to 100 participants on their free tiers. However, Google Meet gives you 60 minutes free (soon to revert), while Zoom limits you to 40 minutes. For paid plans, Google Meet scales to 500 participants, while Zoom can handle up to 1,000 participants with add-ons (Large Meeting add-on). For webinars, Zoom is the clear winner, supporting up to 50,000 view-only attendees with a webinar license.

Screen Sharing and Collaboration

Both platforms offer robust screen sharing. Google Meet integrates seamlessly with Google Slides, Sheets, and Docs, allowing you to present directly from those apps. It also features a "tile" view for up to 49 participants. Zoom offers more granular control, including the ability to share a specific portion of your screen, co-annotate, and use a virtual whiteboard (Zoom Whiteboard).

Breakout Rooms

Zoom's breakout rooms are a standout feature. You can automatically or manually assign participants to smaller groups, and the host can hop between rooms. Google Meet introduced breakout rooms in 2023, but they are only available on paid Workspace plans and lack some of Zoom's flexibility (e.g., pre-assigning participants).

Recording and Transcription

Both platforms offer cloud recording (paid plans) and automatic transcription. Google Meet recordings are saved to Google Drive, making them easy to share and organize. Zoom recordings can be saved to the cloud or locally, and they include a searchable transcript. Zoom's transcription is generally more accurate and includes speaker labels.

Virtual Backgrounds and Filters

Both platforms support virtual backgrounds and filters. Zoom offers a wider variety of built-in backgrounds and the ability to upload custom images and videos. Google Meet provides similar features, including background blur, but its library is smaller. However, Google Meet's AI-powered background noise cancellation is excellent and available on all plans.

User Experience and Ease of Use

Google Meet: Simplicity and Integration

Google Meet is designed for simplicity. If you use Gmail or Google Calendar, you can start or join a meeting with one click. There's no separate app to download (though it's recommended for the best experience). The interface is clean and intuitive, making it ideal for non-technical users. Key benefits include:

  • One-click meeting creation from Calendar or Gmail
  • Seamless integration with Google Chat for in-meeting chat
  • Auto-generated meeting links in Calendar invites
  • No plugins required for most browsers

Zoom: Feature-Rich but Heavier

Zoom requires a desktop app for the full experience, though a browser version is available. The interface is more feature-dense, which can be overwhelming for new users but powerful for power users. Zoom's strength is its flexibility—you can customize virtually every aspect of a meeting, from waiting rooms to recording settings. However, this comes at the cost of a steeper learning curve.

Practical tip: If your team values speed and simplicity, Google Meet is the better choice. If you need advanced controls and don't mind a slightly heavier app, Zoom is superior.

Security and Privacy

Google Meet's Security

Google Meet benefits from Google's world-class security infrastructure. All meetings are encrypted in transit (and at rest for paid plans). Key security features include:

  • Anti-abuse measures that automatically detect and block malicious users
  • No plugins or downloads required (reduces attack surface)
  • Two-factor authentication (2FA) for all Google accounts
  • Meeting codes that are hard to guess (10-character alphanumeric)
  • Host controls to mute, remove, or block participants

Zoom's Security

Zoom had well-publicized security issues in 2020 (e.g., "Zoombombing"), but has since made significant improvements. Today, Zoom offers:

  • End-to-end encryption (E2EE) for all meetings (optional, requires enabling)
  • Waiting rooms, passwords, and meeting locks
  • Advanced admin controls for enterprise accounts
  • SOC 2 Type II and HIPAA compliance

Key takeaway: Both platforms are now highly secure. Google Meet has a slight edge for default security (no extra steps needed), while Zoom offers more granular control for compliance-heavy industries.

Integrations and Ecosystem

Google Meet's Ecosystem Advantage

This is where Google Meet truly shines. If your team uses Google Workspace (Gmail, Calendar, Drive, Docs, Sheets, Slides, Chat), Meet is a natural extension. Key integrations include:

  • Direct meeting creation from Calendar and Gmail
  • Side-by-side viewing of Google Docs, Sheets, and Slides during meetings
  • Auto-save of chat transcripts to Google Chat
  • Recording storage in Google
