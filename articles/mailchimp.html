Mailchimp vs ConvertKit: The Ultimate Showdown for Email Marketing in 2025

Choosing the right email marketing platform can feel like a high-stakes decision for your business. Whether you’re a solopreneur, a blogger, or a growing e-commerce brand, your email list is one of your most valuable assets. Two names frequently come up in this conversation: the industry giant Mailchimp and the creator-focused darling ConvertKit. This detailed comparison will help you decide which tool deserves a spot in your marketing stack, with a special focus on how Mailchimp stacks up against its nimble competitor.

Overview: The Heavyweight vs. The Specialist

Mailchimp is one of the oldest and most recognizable email marketing platforms on the market. Founded in 2001, it has evolved from a simple newsletter tool into a full-fledged marketing platform offering CRM, landing pages, social media ads, and even website building. It’s designed to serve everyone from tiny startups to large enterprises.

ConvertKit, launched in 2013, was built specifically for creators: bloggers, authors, course creators, and YouTubers. Its core philosophy is simplicity and automation, designed to help creators build deep relationships with their audiences without drowning in complexity.

While both can send emails, their philosophies, features, and pricing create very different user experiences. Let’s break down the key areas where Mailchimp and ConvertKit go head-to-head.

Pricing and Value for Money

Mailchimp Pricing

Mailchimp offers a generous free plan that supports up to 500 contacts and 1,000 sends per month. This is a fantastic entry point for beginners. However, as you grow, costs can escalate quickly. Paid plans start at around $13/month for the Essentials plan (500 contacts) and jump significantly as your list grows. One major frustration for many users is that Mailchimp charges based on the number of contacts you have, not just active subscribers, which can inflate costs if you have a large but unengaged list.

ConvertKit Pricing

ConvertKit has a free plan for up to 1,000 subscribers, but it’s limited in features (no automations, no email sequences). Paid plans start at $29/month for up to 1,000 subscribers. This is notably more expensive than Mailchimp at the same subscriber count. However, ConvertKit charges based on the number of paying subscribers, and all features—including visual automations—are unlocked on the first paid tier. For creators who need advanced automation from day one, this can actually be better value.

Verdict: If you’re on a tight budget or just starting out, Mailchimp’s free plan wins. But if you anticipate needing advanced automation and a clean subscriber list, ConvertKit’s pricing model may be more cost-effective in the long run.

Automation Capabilities

Mailchimp’s Automation

Mailchimp has made significant strides in automation in recent years. It offers pre-built customer journeys (e.g., welcome series, abandoned cart emails) and a visual builder for custom automations. However, the logic can feel clunky. Triggers and conditions are limited compared to dedicated automation tools. For example, you cannot easily tag subscribers based on custom events or link multiple automations together without workarounds. The “Classic Automations” feature is straightforward, but the newer “Customer Journeys” can be confusing for new users.

ConvertKit’s Automation

ConvertKit’s automation is its superpower. The visual automation builder is intuitive and powerful. You can tag subscribers, move them between sequences, trigger actions based on link clicks, and create complex rules with ease. The concept of “tags” is central to ConvertKit—you can tag subscribers based on any action, then use those tags to trigger specific sequences. This makes it incredibly easy to segment your audience and send hyper-relevant content. For creators running webinars, courses, or launches, ConvertKit’s automation is a dream.

Verdict: ConvertKit wins hands down for automation flexibility and ease of use. Mailchimp is catching up, but its automation still feels like an add-on rather than a core feature.

Ease of Use and Interface

Mailchimp’s Interface

Mailchimp has undergone several redesigns, and the current interface is modern but can feel overwhelming. There are many menus, options, and settings scattered across the dashboard. Creating a simple email campaign requires navigating through multiple screens. While powerful, the learning curve is steeper than it should be for basic tasks. The drag-and-drop email builder is excellent, though, making it easy to design professional-looking newsletters without coding.

ConvertKit’s Interface

ConvertKit is minimalist by design. The dashboard is clean, with a focus on text-based emails. There is no drag-and-drop email designer—you write in plain text or use basic formatting (bold, italics, headings). This is intentional; ConvertKit believes that creators should focus on content, not design. For bloggers and writers, this is liberating. For brands that need polished, branded emails with images and custom layouts, it can feel limiting.

Verdict: If you want a beautiful, branded email design, Mailchimp is better. If you prefer a distraction-free writing experience and simple navigation, ConvertKit wins.

Email Design and Templates

Mailchimp’s Templates

Mailchimp offers hundreds of pre-designed templates that are responsive and customizable. You can upload your own HTML templates or use the drag-and-drop builder to create stunning emails. The template library includes options for newsletters, promotions, announcements, and more. For businesses that want to maintain a consistent visual brand identity, Mailchimp is the clear choice.

ConvertKit’s Templates

ConvertKit offers a handful of basic templates, but they are text-heavy and simple. You can add images, but the formatting options are limited. There is no drag-and-drop editor. For creators who send long-form content (like blog posts or podcast updates), this is fine. For e-commerce brands or visual businesses, it’s a major drawback.

Verdict: Mailchimp dominates in design flexibility. ConvertKit is for text-first creators.

List Management and Segmentation

Mailchimp’s Segmentation

Mailchimp allows you to segment your audience based on demographics, purchase history, engagement, and custom fields. You can create groups and tags, but the system can be confusing. The “Audience” dashboard is powerful but not intuitive. Many users struggle to find the right segments or accidentally send emails to the wrong group.

ConvertKit’s Segmentation

ConvertKit’s segmentation is built around tags and custom fields. Every subscriber can be tagged based on their actions (e.g., “downloaded freebie,” “attended webinar,” “purchased course”). You can then send targeted emails to any combination of tags. This system is incredibly flexible and easy to understand. You can also use “forms” and “landing pages” to automatically tag new subscribers, making list management almost effortless.

Verdict: ConvertKit’s tag-based system is superior for creators who need granular segmentation. Mailchimp is more complex but also more powerful for advanced demographic segmentation.

Integrations and Ecosystem

Mailchimp’s Integrations

Mailchimp integrates with over 300 apps, including Shopify, WooCommerce, WordPress, Salesforce, and social media platforms. It also has its own CRM, landing pages, and website builder. For e-commerce businesses, Mailchimp offers deep integration with purchase data, enabling features like abandoned cart emails and product recommendations.

ConvertKit’s Integrations

ConvertKit integrates with fewer apps (around 100), but it focuses on the tools that creators actually use: WordPress, Teachable, Thinkific, Gumroad, Zapier, and Stripe. It also has native landing pages and subscription forms. While the ecosystem is smaller, the integrations are deep and reliable. For creators using specific platforms like Teachable, ConvertKit often works better than Mailchimp.

Verdict: Mailchimp wins for sheer number of integrations, especially for e-commerce. ConvertKit wins for creator-focused integrations.
