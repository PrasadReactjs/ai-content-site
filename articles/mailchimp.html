Mailchimp vs ConvertKit: Which Email Marketing Platform is Right for You in 2024?

Choosing the right email marketing platform can make or break your online business. Two heavyweights in the space—Mailchimp and ConvertKit—often top the list for creators, entrepreneurs, and small businesses. But while both tools help you send emails, build audiences, and drive sales, they cater to very different needs. In this detailed comparison, we’ll break down every feature, pricing tier, automation capability, and user experience nuance to help you decide which platform aligns with your goals. By the end, you’ll have actionable insights to either stick with Mailchimp or make the switch to ConvertKit.

Overview: Mailchimp vs ConvertKit

Mailchimp is one of the oldest and most recognizable email marketing platforms, launched in 2001. It’s known for its drag-and-drop email builder, robust analytics, and all-in-one marketing suite that includes landing pages, social media ads, and CRM features. Mailchimp is designed for businesses of all sizes, from e-commerce stores to service providers, and offers a generous free plan.

ConvertKit, on the other hand, is a newer platform (founded in 2013) built specifically for creators, bloggers, and course sellers. Its philosophy is simplicity and powerful automation. ConvertKit focuses on tagging subscribers, visual automation sequences, and selling digital products directly through email. It lacks the flashy templates of Mailchimp but compensates with a text-first, minimalist approach that drives higher engagement.

1. Ease of Use and User Interface

Mailchimp: Feature-Rich but Complex

Mailchimp offers a polished, modern interface but can feel overwhelming for beginners. The dashboard is packed with menus, options, and integrations. Creating a simple email requires navigating through audience segments, templates, and design tools. While the drag-and-drop editor is intuitive for visual layouts, the sheer number of features—like A/B testing, retargeting ads, and e-commerce reports—can distract from the core task of writing an email.

Tip: If you choose Mailchimp, invest time in their free tutorials and use pre-built automation templates to reduce the learning curve.

ConvertKit: Minimalist and Creator-Focused

ConvertKit’s interface is intentionally sparse. You won’t find flashy templates or complex menus. Instead, you get a clean editor that looks like a simple text editor with image placement options. The focus is on writing and sending emails quickly. ConvertKit uses a “subscriber tagging” system instead of lists, which makes segmentation more logical for creators who run multiple offers or content streams.

Actionable advice: If you value speed over design, ConvertKit is your platform. You can send a broadcast in under two minutes from login.

2. Email Templates and Design

Mailchimp: Hundreds of Templates, Fully Customizable

Mailchimp provides over 100 pre-designed email templates, all fully responsive and customizable. You can change colors, fonts, layouts, and add dynamic content like product recommendations. The template builder supports HTML/CSS editing for advanced users. This makes Mailchimp ideal for brands that need visually polished campaigns—think e-commerce newsletters, promotional blasts, or event invitations.

ConvertKit: Text-First with Limited Design Options

ConvertKit deliberately avoids complex templates. It offers a few basic layouts (standard, image left, image right) but no drag-and-drop builder. Emails are primarily text-based, which actually leads to higher open rates and click-through rates for many creators. If you sell courses, coaching, or digital downloads, a simple plain-text email often outperforms a glossy newsletter.

Practical tip: Use ConvertKit if your content is long-form, personal, or educational. Use Mailchimp if you need product showcases, coupons, or branded visuals.

3. Automation and Sequences

Mailchimp: Powerful but Requires Setup

Mailchimp offers robust automation through its “Customer Journey Builder.” You can create complex triggers based on subscriber behavior—like purchase history, email opens, link clicks, and website visits. It integrates seamlessly with e-commerce platforms like Shopify and WooCommerce to send abandoned cart emails, post-purchase follow-ups, and re-engagement campaigns. However, building advanced automations can be time-consuming and may require a paid plan.

ConvertKit: Visual and Intuitive Automation

ConvertKit’s automation builder is its standout feature. You create “visual automations” by dragging and dropping triggers, conditions, and actions. For example, you can tag a subscriber when they click a link, then move them to a sequence, and later send a conditional email based on their purchase status. It’s much easier to set up complex funnels (like email courses or webinar registration sequences) in ConvertKit than in Mailchimp.

Actionable advice: If you run multiple lead magnets, freebies, or paid products, ConvertKit’s tagging system and visual automation will save you hours each week.

4. Pricing: Free vs Paid Plans

Mailchimp: Generous Free Plan, Pricey Upgrades

Mailchimp offers a free plan for up to 500 contacts and 1,000 sends per month. It includes basic templates, a single user, and limited automation. However, to unlock advanced features like A/B testing, multi-step journeys, and phone support, you need to upgrade to the Essentials plan ($13/month) or Standard plan ($20/month). As your list grows, costs can escalate quickly—especially if you need higher send limits or e-commerce integrations.

ConvertKit: No Free Plan, but Fair Pricing for Creators

ConvertKit no longer offers a free plan (it was discontinued in 2023). The Creator plan starts at $29/month for up to 1,000 subscribers. This includes unlimited emails, all automation features, landing pages, and forms. There’s a 14-day free trial. While the entry price is higher than Mailchimp’s free tier, ConvertKit’s paid plans include features that Mailchimp reserves for higher tiers. For example, you get visual automation, tagging, and subscriber scoring from day one.

Tip: If you have fewer than 500 subscribers and need a free option, Mailchimp is the clear winner. If you’re willing to invest in a paid tool from the start, ConvertKit offers better value for creators.

5. Deliverability and Spam Compliance

Mailchimp: Strong Deliverability with Compliance Tools

Mailchimp has a solid reputation for email deliverability. It provides built-in tools like subject line testing, spam score analysis, and content checkers. It also enforces strict compliance with CAN-SPAM laws, including mandatory unsubscribe links and physical address requirements. However, because Mailchimp serves a massive user base, shared IP reputation can sometimes cause issues if other users on the same IP send spam.

ConvertKit: Excellent Deliverability for Personal Emails

ConvertKit’s deliverability is often praised by creators. Because its platform is text-focused and avoids heavy images or HTML, emails are less likely to trigger spam filters. ConvertKit also offers dedicated IP addresses on higher-tier plans, which can improve deliverability for large senders. Their support team is known for helping users troubleshoot deliverability issues quickly.

Actionable advice: Regardless of platform, always warm up your sending domain, clean your list regularly, and avoid spam trigger words. Both Mailchimp and ConvertKit provide tools to help you maintain a healthy sender reputation.

6. Integrations and Ecosystem

Mailchimp: Extensive Third-Party Integrations

Mailchimp integrates with over 300 apps, including Shopify, WooCommerce, WordPress, Salesforce, and Google Analytics. It also offers native integrations with social media platforms like Facebook and Instagram for ad retargeting. If your business relies on multiple tools (e.g., an e-commerce store with a CRM), Mailchimp’s ecosystem is hard to beat.

ConvertKit: Focused Creator Integrations

ConvertKit integrates with popular creator tools like Teachable, Thinkific, Gumroad, WordPress, and Zapier. It also has native integrations with Stripe and PayPal for selling digital products directly. However, its integration library is smaller than Mailchimp’s.
