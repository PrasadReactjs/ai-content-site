Mailchimp vs ConvertKit: The Ultimate Comparison for Email Marketers in 2024

When it comes to email marketing, few debates are as heated as Mailchimp versus ConvertKit. Both platforms have passionate user bases, but they serve very different audiences. Mailchimp, the veteran in the space, has evolved from a simple newsletter tool into an all-in-one marketing platform. ConvertKit, on the other hand, was built from the ground up for creators, bloggers, and course sellers. If you're trying to decide which one is right for your business, this comprehensive comparison will break down every critical factor—from pricing and automation to deliverability and ease of use.

By the end of this article, you'll have a clear roadmap for choosing the platform that aligns with your goals. Let's dive in.

1. Ease of Use and Onboarding

Mailchimp: Feature-Rich but Complex

Mailchimp has undergone several redesigns over the years, and while the interface is polished, it can feel overwhelming for beginners. The platform tries to do everything—email campaigns, landing pages, CRMs, social media scheduling, and even e-commerce integrations. As a result, the learning curve is steeper. New users often find themselves clicking through multiple menus just to send a simple broadcast.

Practical Tip: If you choose Mailchimp, start with the "Classic Builder" for email design, and use their pre-built automation templates to avoid getting lost in the advanced options.

ConvertKit: Simplicity by Design

ConvertKit was built with creators in mind. The dashboard is minimal, clean, and focused on what matters: subscribers, sequences, and forms. There are no distracting bells and whistles. Setting up a welcome sequence or a landing page takes minutes. The tagging system is intuitive—you can tag subscribers based on their actions and then trigger emails accordingly.

Actionable Advice: If you're a solo creator or a small team without a dedicated marketing department, ConvertKit's simplicity will save you hours each week.

2. Email Automation and Sequences

Mailchimp: Powerful but Rigid

Mailchimp offers a visual automation builder with conditional splits, delays, and triggers based on list segments, purchase behavior, and engagement scores. However, the free plan limits automation functionality significantly. On paid plans, you can build complex workflows—like abandoned cart sequences or re-engagement campaigns—but the logic can be clunky. For instance, you cannot easily create "if this, then that" rules without jumping through hoops.

Key Limitation: Mailchimp uses list-based segmentation, not tagging. This means you must move subscribers between lists to change their journey, which can lead to data duplication and confusion.

ConvertKit: Tag-Based Freedom

ConvertKit's automation is built entirely around tags and rules. You can create a visual automation flow where each action (like clicking a link or purchasing a product) adds or removes a tag, triggering a sequence. This is far more flexible than Mailchimp's list-based system. For example, you can have one subscriber with multiple tags (e.g., "freebie downloader," "webinar attendee," "product buyer") and send them a unique sequence based on their combined behavior.

Actionable Advice: Use ConvertKit's "Rules" feature to automate repetitive tasks. For instance, set a rule that automatically tags any new subscriber from a specific landing page, then moves them into a 5-day email sequence.

3. Pricing: Which Platform Offers Better Value?

Mailchimp: Free Plan with Hidden Costs

Mailchimp is famous for its free tier, which supports up to 500 contacts and 1,000 sends per month. However, the free plan includes Mailchimp branding on emails, limited automation, and no phone support. As your list grows, costs escalate quickly. For 1,000 contacts, the Essentials plan starts at $13/month, but to get advanced automation and A/B testing, you'll need the Standard plan at $20/month. For 5,000 contacts, you're looking at $50–$75/month.

Hidden Costs: Mailchimp charges based on the total number of contacts in your audience, not just active subscribers. If you have 2,000 contacts but only 500 are engaged, you still pay for 2,000.

ConvertKit: Creator-Friendly Pricing

ConvertKit offers a free plan for up to 1,000 subscribers, but it includes limited features (no automations, no email support). The Creator plan starts at $29/month for up to 1,000 subscribers and includes everything: unlimited automation, sequences, landing pages, and tagging. For 3,000 subscribers, it's $59/month. While ConvertKit's paid plans start higher than Mailchimp's, they offer more advanced features out of the box without forcing you into higher tiers.

Practical Tip: If you have 1,000–3,000 subscribers and need robust automation, ConvertKit's Creator plan is often cheaper than Mailchimp's Standard plan with similar functionality.

4. Deliverability and Reputation

Mailchimp: Strong but Not Perfect

Mailchimp has a solid deliverability track record, with many users reporting inbox placement rates above 95%. However, because Mailchimp hosts millions of senders, shared IP reputation can sometimes be an issue. If you're on a shared IP and another user sends spam, your deliverability could dip. Mailchimp also has strict anti-spam policies, which can flag legitimate marketers who use certain trigger words.

Actionable Advice: Use Mailchimp's "Clean Contacts" feature regularly to remove unengaged subscribers. This will protect your sender reputation and improve deliverability.

ConvertKit: Built for High Deliverability

ConvertKit takes a more proactive approach to deliverability. They automatically remove bounced emails, enforce double opt-in by default, and provide dedicated IP addresses on higher-tier plans. Many creators report that ConvertKit's deliverability is superior because the platform is smaller and more specialized—there's less noise from spammy senders.

Key Insight: ConvertKit's "Subscriber Scoring" feature lets you track engagement and automatically pause emails to inactive subscribers, which keeps your list healthy and your deliverability high.

5. Templates and Design Flexibility

Mailchimp: Hundreds of Templates

Mailchimp offers a vast library of email templates, from simple newsletters to complex promotional layouts. The drag-and-drop editor is intuitive, but the templates can feel generic. You have full control over colors, fonts, and spacing, but coding custom templates requires familiarity with Mailchimp's proprietary syntax.

Practical Tip: If design is critical for your brand, use Mailchimp's template builder to create a custom layout, then save it as a reusable template. This will save time on future campaigns.

ConvertKit: Minimalist Layouts

ConvertKit does not offer hundreds of templates. Instead, it provides a handful of clean, text-focused layouts. This is intentional—ConvertKit believes that content, not design, drives engagement. If you want fancy graphics, sidebars, or multi-column layouts, ConvertKit will feel limiting. However, for authors, podcasters, and course creators who rely on long-form content, this simplicity is a strength.

Actionable Advice: Use ConvertKit's "Inline" image placement to break up text-heavy emails. Add a simple header image and use bullet points to improve readability.

6. Integrations and Ecosystem

Mailchimp: Extensive but Expensive

Mailchimp integrates with over 300 apps, including Shopify, WooCommerce, Salesforce, and WordPress. However, many advanced integrations (like custom CRM syncs or e-commerce product recommendations) are locked behind the Standard or Premium plans. The free plan supports only basic integrations.

Key Limitation: Mailchimp's API can be complex, and third-party integrations sometimes break after platform updates. You may need a developer to troubleshoot.
