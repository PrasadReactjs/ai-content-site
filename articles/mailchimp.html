Mailchimp vs ConvertKit: The Ultimate Comparison for Email Marketers in 2024

When it comes to email marketing, few names are as recognizable as Mailchimp. For over two decades, Mailchimp has been the go-to platform for businesses of all sizes, from solopreneurs to enterprise teams. But in recent years, ConvertKit has emerged as a powerful competitor, particularly for creators, bloggers, and course sellers. If you're trying to decide which platform is right for you, this detailed comparison will help you weigh the pros, cons, and key differences. We'll cover pricing, features, ease of use, automation, deliverability, and more—all while keeping Mailchimp as our central reference point.

1. Overview: What Each Platform Does Best

Mailchimp is a full-featured marketing platform that goes beyond email. It includes landing pages, social media ads, postcards, and even a basic CRM. It's designed for ecommerce, small businesses, and marketers who want an all-in-one solution. ConvertKit, on the other hand, is laser-focused on email marketing for creators. It's built for bloggers, authors, podcasters, and online course creators who need simplicity and powerful automation for nurturing subscribers.

2. Pricing: Mailchimp vs ConvertKit

Mailchimp Pricing

  • Free Plan: Up to 500 contacts, 1,000 sends/month, basic templates, and limited automation. Great for testing the waters.
  • Essentials Plan: Starts at $13/month (500 contacts). Adds A/B testing, custom templates, and 3 seats.
  • Standard Plan: Starts at $20/month (500 contacts). Includes customer journey builder, retargeting, and advanced segmentation.
  • Premium Plan: Starts at $350/month (10,000+ contacts). Offers phone support, multivariate testing, and advanced reporting.

ConvertKit Pricing

  • Free Plan: Up to 1,000 contacts, unlimited emails, but limited to basic forms and landing pages. No automation sequences.
  • Creator Plan: Starts at $29/month (1,000 contacts). Includes all features: automation, tags, segments, and subscriber scoring.
  • Creator Pro: Starts at $59/month (1,000 contacts). Adds newsletter referral system, advanced reporting, and priority support.

Winner for budget: Mailchimp's free plan is more generous for small lists (500 contacts vs 1,000 for ConvertKit, but ConvertKit's free plan is unlimited on email sends). However, as your list grows, ConvertKit's pricing becomes more expensive per contact. For example, at 5,000 contacts, Mailchimp Standard costs about $69/month, while ConvertKit Creator costs $59/month. At 10,000 contacts, Mailchimp Standard is $99/month, ConvertKit Creator is $79/month. Mailchimp often wins for larger lists on a tight budget.

3. Ease of Use and Interface

Mailchimp has a modern, colorful interface with drag-and-drop email builders. However, its many features can feel overwhelming for beginners. The navigation is not always intuitive—finding specific settings (like automation triggers or audience segments) sometimes requires clicking through several menus. ConvertKit is deliberately minimal. Its text-based editor emphasizes writing over design, which appeals to creators who want to send clean, personal emails without fuss. The visual automation builder in ConvertKit is straightforward: you see a linear flow of triggers and actions. Mailchimp's customer journey builder is more powerful but also more complex.

Tip: If you prioritize speed and simplicity, ConvertKit wins. If you need rich design options and a visual builder, Mailchimp is better.

4. Email Design and Templates

Mailchimp offers hundreds of responsive templates, plus a flexible drag-and-drop editor. You can create branded emails with custom layouts, images, and buttons. It also supports custom CSS for advanced users. ConvertKit takes a different approach: it offers only a few basic templates (mostly text-based with optional images). The emphasis is on content, not design. This is intentional—ConvertKit's research shows that plain-text emails often have higher engagement rates. But if you need visually stunning newsletters or promotional emails, Mailchimp is the clear winner.

5. Automation and Sequences

Mailchimp Automation

  • Customer Journey Builder: Visual, drag-and-drop automation with triggers (e.g., signup, purchase, date-based), conditions, and actions.
  • Pre-built automations: Welcome series, abandoned cart, product recommendations, and more.
  • Advanced segmentation: Combine tags, custom fields, and purchase behavior.

ConvertKit Automation

  • Visual sequences: Simple, linear automation with triggers (tag added, form submitted, purchase) and actions (send email, tag, move to sequence).
  • Rules: If-then logic for branching paths based on subscriber actions.
  • Tag-based system: Very flexible—tags act as both labels and triggers.

Which is better? For complex, multi-step ecommerce automations (e.g., "If they bought product A, send series B; if they abandoned cart, send series C"), Mailchimp's journey builder is more robust. For simple, content-driven sequences (e.g., "Send a 5-day email course"), ConvertKit's sequences are easier to set up and maintain.

6. Segmentation and Tagging

Mailchimp uses groups, segments, and tags. Groups are broad categories (e.g., "Customer Type"), segments are dynamic filters (e.g., "opened email in last 30 days"), and tags are flexible labels. The segmentation logic is powerful but can be confusing because of the multiple systems. ConvertKit relies almost entirely on tags. Every subscriber can have multiple tags, and you can create segments based on tag combinations, custom fields, or activity. It's simpler and more intuitive for creators who think in terms of "what topics is this subscriber interested in?"

Actionable advice: If you sell physical products and need to segment by purchase history, Mailchimp is stronger. If you run a blog or podcast and tag subscribers by content preferences, ConvertKit is easier.

7. Deliverability and Reliability

Both platforms have good deliverability, but Mailchimp benefits from years of infrastructure and relationships with ISPs. Its built-in tools for cleaning lists and managing bounces are robust. ConvertKit also has strong deliverability, partly because its text-heavy emails often avoid spam filters. However, ConvertKit's smaller user base means fewer reputation issues. In practice, both platforms achieve 95-99% deliverability rates when best practices are followed (e.g., using double opt-in, avoiding spammy language, sending consistently).

8. Integrations and Ecosystem

Mailchimp integrates with over 300 apps, including Shopify, WooCommerce, WordPress, Salesforce, and Facebook. It also has its own CRM and social media ad tools. ConvertKit has fewer integrations (around 100+), but they are carefully chosen for creators: WordPress, Teachable, Gumroad, Stripe, Zapier, and more. ConvertKit also has a native landing page builder and a "newsletter referral system" (great for growing your list). If you rely on a specific ecommerce or CRM platform, check compatibility first—but for most creators, ConvertKit's integrations are sufficient.

9. Reporting and Analytics

Mailchimp provides detailed reports: open rates, click rates, revenue per email, click maps, and even social media engagement. The Premium plan adds multivariate testing and advanced segmentation reporting. ConvertKit offers simpler reports: open rates, click rates, unsubscribes, and subscriber growth. The Pro plan adds "subscriber scoring" (identifying your most engaged readers) and "link tracking" (which links get clicked). For data-driven marketers, Mailchimp is superior. For creators who just want to know "did my email perform well?" ConvertKit is enough.
