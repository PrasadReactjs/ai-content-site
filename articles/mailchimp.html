Mailchimp vs ConvertKit: Which Email Marketing Platform is Right for You?

When it comes to email marketing, two names frequently dominate the conversation: Mailchimp and ConvertKit. While both platforms help you build audiences, send campaigns, and automate workflows, they cater to very different needs. If you're searching for a detailed comparison to decide between these two giants, you've come to the right place. In this guide, we'll break down features, pricing, usability, and scalability—with a special focus on how Mailchimp stacks up against ConvertKit in 2025.

Whether you're a solopreneur, a growing e‑commerce brand, or a content creator, understanding these differences will save you time, money, and frustration. Let's dive in.

Quick Overview: Mailchimp vs ConvertKit

Before we get into the nitty‑gritty, here's a high‑level summary:

  • Mailchimp is an all‑in‑one marketing platform designed for small to medium businesses. It offers email marketing, landing pages, social media ads, postcards, and a powerful CRM—all under one roof.
  • ConvertKit is a creator‑focused email marketing tool built for bloggers, authors, course creators, and influencers. It prioritizes simplicity, subscriber tagging, and automation over flashy templates and multi‑channel marketing.

Ease of Use and Onboarding

Mailchimp

Mailchimp has a modern, intuitive interface that has improved significantly over the years. Its drag‑and‑drop email builder is robust, offering hundreds of templates (both free and premium). However, because Mailchimp packs so many features, new users may feel overwhelmed. The onboarding wizard helps, but you'll need time to explore the dashboard, audience management, and campaign settings.

ConvertKit

ConvertKit takes a minimalist approach. Its editor is text‑based (no drag‑and‑drop), which means less design flexibility but faster email creation. The learning curve is shallow—you can send your first broadcast within minutes. ConvertKit's strength lies in its "visual automations," which let you map out subscriber journeys without coding.

Practical Tip: If you want beautiful, branded emails with lots of design options, start with Mailchimp. If you value speed and simplicity, ConvertKit wins.

Email Design and Templates

Mailchimp

Mailchimp offers over 100 professionally designed email templates, fully customizable via a drag‑and‑drop editor. You can also code your own HTML emails. The platform supports advanced features like dynamic content (showing different content to different subscribers) and A/B testing for subject lines, content, and send times.

ConvertKit

ConvertKit does not have a drag‑and‑drop builder. Instead, it uses a simple text editor with inline image placement. You can add basic formatting, buttons, and custom HTML, but the design options are limited. ConvertKit's philosophy is that content matters more than design—so emails look clean and consistent across devices.

Actionable Advice: For e‑commerce brands that rely on visual merchandising, Mailchimp is the clear winner. For writers and course creators who send text‑heavy newsletters, ConvertKit's simplicity is a plus.

Audience Management and Segmentation

Mailchimp

Mailchimp uses "Audience" as its core data structure. You can create segments based on tags, purchase history, engagement, location, and custom fields. Its CRM features allow you to track contact interactions across email, social media, and your website. However, tagging in Mailchimp can be less intuitive than in ConvertKit—tags are often mixed with groups and segments, which can confuse new users.

ConvertKit

ConvertKit's audience management is its superpower. Every subscriber gets a profile where you can assign unlimited tags and custom fields. Tags are the backbone of automation—you can trigger emails, sequences, and automations based on tag additions or removals. ConvertKit also offers "subscriber scoring" to identify your most engaged fans.

Practical Tip: If your strategy relies on deep behavioral tagging (e.g., "downloaded freebie," "attended webinar," "purchased course"), ConvertKit is more straightforward. For broad demographic or geographic segmentation, Mailchimp works well.

Automation and Workflows

Mailchimp

Mailchimp offers a visual automation builder called "Customer Journey Builder." You can create multi‑step automations triggered by subscriber actions (e.g., email opens, link clicks, purchases). The builder supports conditional splits, delays, and actions like adding tags or updating CRM records. However, complex workflows can become clunky, and the logic sometimes feels less flexible than dedicated automation tools.

ConvertKit

ConvertKit's automation is where it truly shines. Its visual builder is clean and logical—you can create sequences, rules, and funnels with ease. For example, you can set up a "welcome sequence" that tags subscribers based on which link they clicked, then moves them to different email sequences. ConvertKit also offers "visual automations" that let you see the entire subscriber journey at a glance.

Actionable Advice: For simple autoresponders (welcome emails, abandoned cart reminders), both tools work fine. For advanced, multi‑branch automations (e.g., quiz funnels, multi‑step product launches), ConvertKit is more reliable and easier to debug.

Pricing and Value for Money

Mailchimp

Mailchimp offers a free plan (up to 500 contacts and 1,000 sends/month) which is great for beginners. Paid plans start at $13/month (Essentials), $20/month (Standard), and $350/month (Premium). Pricing scales with contact count and features. One common complaint: as your list grows, costs can skyrocket—and you may pay for features you don't use.

ConvertKit

ConvertKit has a free plan (up to 1,000 subscribers, but with limited features). Paid plans start at $29/month for up to 1,000 subscribers (Creator plan), and $59/month (Creator Pro) which adds advanced reporting and newsletter referral system. ConvertKit's pricing is simpler—no hidden fees for automation or segmentation—but it's generally more expensive than Mailchimp at the entry level.

Practical Tip: If you're on a tight budget and need a free plan, Mailchimp is the better start. If you have 1,000+ engaged subscribers and prioritize automation, ConvertKit's higher price may be worth it.

Integrations and Ecosystem

Mailchimp

Mailchimp integrates with over 300 apps, including Shopify, WooCommerce, WordPress, Salesforce, and Facebook Ads. Its native integrations with e‑commerce platforms are particularly strong—you can sync purchase data, send abandoned cart emails, and track revenue. Mailchimp also offers a built‑in CRM and social media ad management, reducing the need for extra tools.

ConvertKit

ConvertKit integrates with fewer apps (around 100), but focuses on creator‑friendly tools like Teachable, Thinkific, Gumroad, WordPress, and Zapier. It also has a native "Creator Network" for newsletter recommendations. ConvertKit's API is developer‑friendly, so custom integrations are possible.

Actionable Advice: For e‑commerce stores, Mailchimp is the stronger choice due to its deep Shopify and WooCommerce integrations. For course creators and membership sites, ConvertKit's integrations with learning platforms make it a natural fit.

Deliverability and Analytics

Mailchimp

Mailchimp has good deliverability, but some users report emails landing in spam folders due to shared IP addresses on lower plans. Its analytics include open rates, click rates, unsubscribes, bounces, and revenue tracking. The "Comparative Reports" feature lets you benchmark your performance against industry averages.

ConvertKit

ConvertKit prides itself on high deliverability—its infrastructure is built for transactional and promotional emails. Analytics are simpler: open rates, click rates, unsubscribes, and subscriber growth. ConvertKit also offers "link tracking" and "subscriber scoring" to gauge engagement.
