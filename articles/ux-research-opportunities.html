Your Ultimate Guide to Finding the Best UX Research Opportunities

The field of User Experience (UX) research has exploded in recent years. As companies increasingly recognize that user-centered design drives product success, the demand for skilled UX researchers has soared. Whether you are a seasoned professional looking for your next challenge or a newcomer eager to break into the industry, knowing where to find UX research opportunities is the first and most crucial step.

This guide curates the top job boards, provides actionable strategies for your job search, and offers practical tips to help you land your dream role. We’ll cover everything from niche platforms to networking tactics, ensuring you have a comprehensive toolkit for navigating the UX research job market.

Why Specialized Job Boards Matter for UX Researchers

General job boards like Indeed or LinkedIn can be overwhelming, with thousands of irrelevant listings. For a specialized field like UX research, niche job boards offer several advantages:

  • Targeted Listings: You only see roles that require your specific skill set (e.g., usability testing, ethnography, survey design).
  • Higher Quality Employers: Companies posting on these boards genuinely understand and value UX research.
  • Community Insights: Many boards feature salary data, company reviews, and forums where researchers share tips.
  • Reduced Competition: You are competing with a smaller, more relevant pool of candidates.

Top Curated Job Boards for UX Research Opportunities

Below, we’ve curated the most effective platforms for finding UX research opportunities. Bookmark these and check them regularly.

1. The UX Research Job Board (UXRJobs.com)

This is the gold standard for UX research listings. It is exclusively dedicated to UX researcher roles, from junior to director levels. The site also features a comprehensive salary database and a community forum where you can ask for advice on portfolios or interview prep. Many remote and hybrid opportunities are posted here daily.

2. Authentic Jobs

While not exclusively UX research, Authentic Jobs has a dedicated “UX & Research” category. It is known for high-quality postings from design-led companies and startups. The site filters out spam and low-quality listings, saving you time.

3. UX Design Jobs (by UX Design Institute)

This board includes a robust “Research” filter. It is particularly strong for European and UK-based opportunities, though it also features global remote roles. The accompanying blog often publishes articles on interview tips and portfolio building for researchers.

4. Dribbble / Behance Jobs

While primarily for visual designers, many companies now post “UX Researcher” or “User Researcher” roles here. The advantage is that you can view the company’s design culture through their portfolio. Use the search filter to narrow down to “Research” or “UX.”

5. LinkedIn (with Smart Filters)

Don’t ignore LinkedIn entirely. Use advanced search filters: set the job title to “User Experience Researcher,” “UX Researcher,” or “User Researcher.” Then, filter by “Under 10 applicants” (if available) or use the “Posted in the last 24 hours” filter to find fresh UX research opportunities. Follow companies like Google, Meta, Microsoft, and IDEO, which often post directly on their LinkedIn career pages.

6. Indeed / Glassdoor (Niche Search Strings)

For general boards, use specific search strings to cut through noise. Try: “UX researcher” AND “usability” NOT “designer” or “user research” AND “mixed methods”. Glassdoor is excellent for researching company culture and salary expectations before applying.

7. We Work Remotely & Remote OK

If you are seeking remote UX research opportunities, these boards are essential. Use the search term “UX research” or “user research.” Many fully remote startups post here, offering flexible schedules and global teams.

Actionable Tips to Land UX Research Roles

Finding the listing is only half the battle. Here are practical strategies to increase your success rate.

Optimize Your Resume and Portfolio

  • Use Keywords: Incorporate terms like “usability testing,” “A/B testing,” “user interviews,” “affinity mapping,” “journey mapping,” and “qualitative research” throughout your resume.
  • Show Impact: Instead of listing tasks, show results. For example: “Conducted 20 user interviews that led to a 30% reduction in onboarding friction.”
  • Portfolio Structure: For each project, include: Problem → Research Method → Key Findings → Design Recommendations → Business Impact. Recruiters love seeing this clear narrative.

Network Strategically

  • Attend UX Research Meetups: Look for groups like “UX Research & Strategy” on Meetup.com. Virtual events are great for networking across geographies.
  • Engage on LinkedIn: Comment thoughtfully on posts from UX research leaders (e.g., Erika Hall, Steve Portigal). Share your own insights or case studies.
  • Join Slack Communities: Groups like “UX Research Collective” or “Research Ops” often have dedicated #jobs channels with exclusive postings.

Prepare for the Interview Process

  • Know Your Methods: Be ready to explain when to use a diary study vs. a usability test vs. a survey. Companies want researchers who can choose the right tool for the question.
  • Case Study Presentation: Most interviews require you to present a past project. Practice telling a compelling story: What was the business goal? How did you recruit participants? What was the most surprising finding?
  • Ask Smart Questions: Inquire about the company’s research maturity, how findings are shared with stakeholders, and what tools the team uses (e.g., Dovetail, UserTesting, Lookback).

Emerging Trends in UX Research Hiring

Staying ahead of trends can give you a competitive edge. Here’s what’s shaping the current job market:

  • Mixed Methods in Demand: Employers increasingly want researchers who can handle both qualitative (interviews, ethnography) and quantitative (surveys, analytics) data.
  • Research Operations (ResearchOps): Roles focused on streamlining research processes, managing participant panels, and tooling are growing. If you enjoy system-building, consider this niche.
  • AI and UX Research: Knowing how to use AI tools for data analysis (e.g., sentiment analysis, transcription) is a differentiator. Some companies are hiring “AI UX Researchers” to study human-AI interaction.
  • Remote-First Culture: While some companies are returning to office, many remain remote or hybrid. Highlight your ability to conduct research remotely (e.g., using remote testing tools, managing asynchronous studies).

Conclusion: Your Next Step Starts Now

The hunt for UX research opportunities can feel daunting, but with the right curated job boards and a strategic approach, you can find a role that aligns with your skills and passions. Start by bookmarking the boards listed above, updating your portfolio to highlight impact, and engaging with the UX research community.

Call to Action: Don’t just read this guide—act on it. Today, pick one job board you haven’t used before (try UXRJobs.com if you haven’t yet) and apply to one role that genuinely excites you. Then, join one UX research Slack community or LinkedIn group. The next great UX research opportunity is waiting—go get it.
