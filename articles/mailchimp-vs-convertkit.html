Mailchimp vs ConvertKit: The Ultimate Email Marketing Showdown

Choosing the right email marketing platform can make or break your online business. Whether you're a blogger, course creator, or e-commerce store owner, your email list is your most valuable asset. Two of the most popular platforms on the market—Mailchimp and ConvertKit—often go head-to-head, but they serve very different audiences. In this comprehensive comparison, we’ll break down automation, deliverability, pricing, and templates to help you decide which platform deserves your investment. If you’ve been searching for a definitive mailchimp vs convertkit guide, you’ve come to the right place.

1. Automation: Ease of Use vs Power

Mailchimp Automation

Mailchimp offers a visual automation builder that’s user-friendly for beginners. You can create simple workflows like welcome sequences, abandoned cart emails, and birthday campaigns. However, the free plan limits you to basic triggers (e.g., signup, purchase, date). For more advanced automation—like conditional splits or multi-step funnels—you’ll need to upgrade to the Standard or Premium plans. Mailchimp’s automation is robust but can feel clunky when you try to build complex sequences.

ConvertKit Automation

ConvertKit was built for creators who need sophisticated automation without coding. Its visual builder is clean and intuitive. You can tag subscribers based on behavior, create custom rules, and build elaborate sequences in minutes. ConvertKit’s “visual automations” allow you to see the entire subscriber journey at a glance. The platform excels at conditional logic—for example, sending different emails based on whether a subscriber clicked a link or opened a previous email. For course launches, membership sites, or multi-product funnels, ConvertKit is the clear winner.

Verdict: If you need simple automations and a low learning curve, Mailchimp works. For advanced, creator-focused automation, ConvertKit wins hands down.

2. Deliverability: Getting Your Emails to the Inbox

Deliverability is the single most important factor in email marketing. A beautiful email that lands in spam is worthless. Both platforms have strong reputations, but there are key differences.

Mailchimp Deliverability

Mailchimp has historically struggled with deliverability for high-volume senders. Because it serves a massive user base (including many spammers), its IP reputation can be inconsistent. Mailchimp uses a shared IP infrastructure on lower plans, which means your deliverability is partly tied to other senders. They do offer dedicated IPs on Premium plans, but that’s expensive. Many users report that Mailchimp emails land in Promotions tab (Gmail) rather than the primary inbox.

ConvertKit Deliverability

ConvertKit prioritizes deliverability from day one. They use a dedicated sending infrastructure and actively monitor sender reputation. ConvertKit’s team works with major ISPs (Gmail, Outlook, Yahoo) to ensure high inbox placement. They also provide detailed analytics on open rates, click rates, and spam complaints. Creators who rely on email for sales often report better open and click-through rates with ConvertKit. The platform also supports custom DKIM and SPF records for authentication.

Verdict: ConvertKit consistently outperforms Mailchimp in deliverability, especially for active senders. If your business depends on emails reaching inboxes, ConvertKit is the safer bet.

3. Pricing: Free vs Value

Pricing is often the deciding factor for small businesses and solopreneurs. Let’s break down the numbers.

Mailchimp Pricing

  • Free Plan: Up to 500 contacts, 1,000 sends/month, basic templates, and limited automation. Good for testing the waters.
  • Essentials: $13/month (500 contacts). Adds more templates, A/B testing, and basic automation.
  • Standard: $20/month (500 contacts). Includes advanced automation, retargeting, and custom templates.
  • Premium: $350/month (up to 10,000 contacts). Dedicated IP, phone support, and advanced segmentation.

Mailchimp’s pricing scales quickly as your list grows. For 5,000 contacts on the Standard plan, you’re looking at $45/month. Also, Mailchimp charges you for contacts even if they’re unsubscribed (up to a limit).

ConvertKit Pricing

  • Free Plan: Up to 1,000 contacts, unlimited sends, but limited to basic email forms and landing pages. No automation or tagging.
  • Creator Plan: $29/month (up to 1,000 contacts). Includes all features—automation, tags, sequences, analytics, and unlimited emails.
  • Creator Pro: $59/month (up to 1,000 contacts). Adds subscriber scoring, advanced reporting, and third-party integrations.

ConvertKit’s pricing is simpler and more transparent. You pay for active subscribers only—unsubscribed contacts are not counted. For 5,000 contacts, the Creator plan is $79/month. While ConvertKit has no true free tier (the free plan is very limited), its paid plans offer more value for serious creators.

Verdict: Mailchimp is cheaper for very small lists (under 500) thanks to its free plan. For growing lists (1,000+), ConvertKit often provides better value per dollar, especially when you factor in deliverability and automation.

4. Templates and Design: Flexibility vs Simplicity

Mailchimp Templates

Mailchimp offers hundreds of pre-designed templates, both for email and landing pages. You can customize colors, fonts, and layouts using a drag-and-drop editor. Mailchimp also supports custom HTML templates. However, the templates can feel outdated and “template-y.” They’re great for standard newsletters but less ideal for unique brand identities. Mailchimp’s email builder is powerful but can be overwhelming with too many options.

ConvertKit Templates

ConvertKit takes a minimalist approach. There are fewer pre-built templates, and they are text-heavy with clean, simple designs. This is intentional—ConvertKit believes that plain-text emails and simple layouts outperform flashy designs in terms of engagement. You can add images, buttons, and custom CSS, but the builder is not drag-and-drop. For creators who prioritize content over design, this is a plus. For visual brands (e.g., fashion, design), it may feel limiting.

Verdict: If you need beautiful, customizable templates with a visual editor, choose Mailchimp. If you prefer simple, high-converting text-based emails, ConvertKit is better.

5. Practical Tips and Actionable Advice

Here are some tips to get the most out of whichever platform you choose:

  • For Mailchimp users: Clean your list regularly. Remove inactive subscribers to improve deliverability. Use A/B testing on subject lines and send times. Upgrade to a paid plan as soon as you hit 500 contacts to unlock automation.
  • For ConvertKit users: Leverage tagging and segmentation from day one. Tag subscribers based on their interests, purchases, or behavior. Use sequences for lead magnets and courses. Always track open and click rates to refine your strategy.
  • General advice: Regardless of platform, warm up your sending domain if you’re new. Send consistently, avoid spammy language, and always provide an easy unsubscribe link.

6. Which Platform Should You Choose?

The answer depends on your goals:

  • Choose Mailchimp if: You’re a beginner with a small list (under 500), you need a free plan, or you rely heavily on visual email templates. Mailchimp is also good for e-commerce stores using Shopify or WooCommerce.
  • Choose ConvertKit if: You’re a blogger, course creator, author, or influencer. You need advanced automation, high deliverability, and a platform that grows with you. ConvertKit is ideal if you sell digital products or run membership sites.

Conclusion

Both Mailchimp and ConvertKit are excellent email marketing platforms, but they serve different niches. Mailchimp is a jack-of-all-trades with a generous free plan, while ConvertKit is a specialist tool built for creators who need automation and deliverability. In the mailchimp vs convertkit debate, there’s no one-size-fits-all answer.

If you’re serious about building an online business, I recommend starting with ConvertKit.
