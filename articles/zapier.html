Zapier vs Make: The Ultimate Automation Showdown for 2024

In the rapidly evolving world of workflow automation, two names dominate the conversation: Zapier and Make (formerly Integromat). Both platforms promise to connect your apps, automate repetitive tasks, and save you hours of manual work—but they take fundamentally different approaches. If you're searching for the right tool to streamline your business processes, this comprehensive comparison will help you decide which platform aligns with your needs. Let's dive into the details of Zapier vs Make, covering features, pricing, ease of use, and advanced capabilities.

What Are Zapier and Make?

Zapier is the industry veteran, launched in 2011, known for its simplicity and massive app integration library. It uses "Zaps" (automated workflows) that follow a simple "if this, then that" logic. Make, rebranded from Integromat in 2021, takes a more visual and flexible approach, allowing users to build complex automations with a drag-and-drop editor that resembles a flowchart.

Both tools serve the same core purpose—connecting apps without coding—but their philosophies differ. Zapier prioritizes ease of use for beginners, while Make appeals to power users who need granular control over data manipulation and multi-step logic.

Key Differences at a Glance

  • Interface: Zapier uses a linear, step-by-step setup; Make uses a visual canvas with nodes and branches.
  • Complexity: Zapier is best for simple, linear workflows; Make excels at complex, multi-branch automations.
  • Data manipulation: Make offers built-in tools for filtering, transforming, and aggregating data; Zapier relies on third-party integrations or limited built-in options.
  • Pricing: Zapier's free tier is generous but limits tasks; Make's free tier includes more operations but has a steeper learning curve.
  • App integrations: Both support 5,000+ apps, but Zapier has a slight edge in popular business tools.

Detailed Feature Comparison

1. Ease of Use

Zapier is designed for non-technical users. Its interface is clean and intuitive: you choose a trigger app and event, then select an action app and event. The platform automatically maps fields between apps, and you can test each step instantly. For most common tasks—like saving email attachments to Google Drive or creating Trello cards from new Gmail messages—Zapier requires zero learning curve.

Make, on the other hand, presents a blank canvas with modules that you connect manually. You need to understand terms like "routers," "filters," and "aggregators." While this flexibility is powerful, it can overwhelm beginners. However, Make provides excellent documentation and templates to get started.

Verdict: If you want to automate in minutes, choose Zapier. If you're willing to invest time in learning, Make offers more control.

2. Automation Logic and Flexibility

Here's where the platforms diverge most. Zapier supports linear Zaps with up to 3 steps on the free plan and up to 100 steps on paid plans. You can add filters, delays, and paths (conditional logic), but branching is limited. For example, you can create a Zap that sends a Slack message if a lead is "hot" and an email if "cold," but managing complex scenarios requires multiple Zaps.

Make shines with its visual builder. You can create automations with multiple branches, loops, and data aggregation in a single scenario. For instance, you could fetch all new Shopify orders, check inventory for each item, send personalized emails to customers, and update a Google Sheet—all in one visual flow. Make also supports webhooks, custom APIs, and error handling at a granular level.

Practical tip: If your workflows involve conditional logic, data transformation (e.g., converting currencies), or connecting multiple data sources, Make is the superior choice. For simple "move data from A to B" tasks, Zapier is faster to set up.

3. App Integrations and Ecosystem

Both platforms boast 5,000+ integrations. Zapier has a slight advantage in depth—it often supports more specific triggers and actions within popular apps like Salesforce, HubSpot, and Gmail. For example, Zapier can trigger on "new email with attachment" in Gmail, while Make may require a custom webhook for the same.

Make, however, excels at connecting apps that Zapier doesn't support natively. Its HTTP module and advanced data manipulation tools allow you to work with any REST API, effectively making its integration library limitless. This is a game-changer for developers or businesses using niche software.

Actionable advice: Before choosing, check which platform offers the specific triggers and actions you need. Use each tool's "App Directory" to verify. For most standard business tools (Slack, Mailchimp, Google Workspace), both work well.

4. Pricing and Value

Zapier pricing is task-based. The free plan includes 100 tasks/month and 5 Zaps. Paid plans start at $19.99/month for 750 tasks and 20 Zaps. Premium apps (like Salesforce or HubSpot) require higher-tier plans. Tasks are counted per-step, so a 5-step Zap consumes 5 tasks per run.

Make uses an operation-based model. The free plan includes 1,000 operations/month and unlimited scenarios. Paid plans start at $9/month for 10,000 operations. Operations are counted per module execution, making it more cost-effective for complex workflows. For example, a scenario with 10 modules uses 10 operations per run.

Cost comparison example: If you run a 3-step automation 500 times per month, Zapier would consume 1,500 tasks (costing ~$30/month on the Professional plan). Make would consume 1,500 operations (fitting within the $9/month plan). For heavy users, Make is significantly cheaper.

5. Advanced Features

Zapier offers "Zapier Tables" (a built-in database) and "Zapier Interfaces" (a tool to build custom forms and dashboards). These are useful for creating simple apps without coding. However, they're still maturing.

Make provides built-in tools for data transformation (like "Text parser" for regex), error handling (re-routing failed operations), and scheduling (run scenarios on cron-like intervals). It also supports "scenario templates" that you can clone and modify. For developers, Make's "Webhook" and "HTTP" modules allow unlimited customization.

When to Choose Zapier

  • You're a beginner who needs quick automations without technical jargon.
  • Your workflows are linear (e.g., "when X happens, do Y").
  • You use popular apps like Gmail, Slack, or HubSpot and need deep integration.
  • You prefer a polished, user-friendly interface with excellent customer support.
  • You need pre-built templates for common use cases (Zapier has thousands).

When to Choose Make

  • You have complex, multi-step workflows with conditional logic and loops.
  • You need granular data manipulation (filtering, transforming, aggregating).
  • You're on a tight budget and need high-volume automation at low cost.
  • You're a developer or power user comfortable with visual programming.
  • You need to connect niche APIs that lack native integration.

Practical Tips for Both Platforms

  1. Start with a template: Both platforms offer pre-built workflows. Use them to learn the interface before building from scratch.
  2. Test thoroughly: Always run a test with dummy data to ensure your automation behaves as expected.
  3. Monitor usage: Track your task/operation consumption to avoid unexpected charges. Both platforms provide dashboards.
  4. Use filters wisely: Add filters early in your workflow to skip unnecessary steps, saving both time and costs.
