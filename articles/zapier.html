Zapier vs Make: The Ultimate Comparison for Automation in 2024

In the rapidly evolving world of workflow automation, two platforms consistently dominate the conversation: Zapier and Make (formerly Integromat). Businesses of all sizes rely on these tools to connect apps, automate repetitive tasks, and streamline operations. But which one is right for you? This detailed comparison will help you decide by examining features, pricing, ease of use, and real-world applications.

What Is Zapier?

Zapier is the original no-code automation platform, launched in 2011. It connects over 6,000 apps through simple triggers and actions, called "Zaps." Its strength lies in its user-friendly interface and massive app library, making it ideal for non-technical users who want quick integrations.

What Is Make?

Make (formerly Integromat) is a visual automation platform that emphasizes complex, multi-step workflows. It offers a drag-and-drop editor with conditional logic, loops, and data transformation capabilities. Make is often favored by power users and developers who need granular control over their automations.

Key Differences at a Glance

  • Ease of Use: Zapier wins for beginners with its linear, step-by-step setup. Make has a steeper learning curve due to its visual canvas.
  • Complexity: Make excels at advanced scenarios with filters, routers, and data aggregation. Zapier is better for simple, linear workflows.
  • Pricing: Zapier's free tier is limited to 100 tasks/month; Make's free tier offers 1,000 operations/month.
  • App Integrations: Zapier leads with 6,000+ apps; Make has around 2,000+ but includes deep API access.

Detailed Feature Comparison

1. Workflow Builder

Zapier uses a straightforward "if this, then that" logic. You select a trigger app (e.g., "New Gmail email"), set conditions, and choose an action (e.g., "Create Trello card"). This linear approach is perfect for simple tasks like sending Slack notifications for new form submissions.

Make offers a visual canvas where you drag and connect modules. You can create branches, loops, and data transformations. For example, you can pull data from a Google Sheet, filter rows based on conditions, and send personalized emails via SendGrid—all in one visual flow.

2. App Integrations

Zapier's library is unmatched. With 6,000+ integrations, you'll find almost any popular SaaS app—Salesforce, Shopify, HubSpot, Google Workspace, and more. It also offers premium integrations for enterprise tools.

Make has 2,000+ apps but compensates with robust API modules. You can connect custom endpoints, use HTTP requests, and parse JSON/XML data. This makes Make superior for developers needing deep integrations.

3. Data Transformation

Zapier includes basic data formatting (e.g., date formatting, text splitting) via its "Formatter" tool. For advanced needs, you'd need to use Zapier's "Code" step (Python or JavaScript).

Make offers built-in data transformation tools: aggregators, array functions, and text parsers. You can merge data from multiple sources, convert formats, and perform calculations without writing code.

4. Error Handling & Monitoring

Zapier provides a simple task history with status indicators (success, failure, held). You can set up error notifications via email or Slack, but advanced retry logic is limited.

Make includes detailed execution logs, error handling routes (e.g., "On error" modules), and automatic retries. You can also create "rollback" scenarios to undo actions if a workflow fails.

5. Pricing Models

Zapier Pricing (2024):

  • Free: 100 tasks/month, 5 Zaps, 1-step workflows
  • Starter: $19.99/month (750 tasks, 3-step workflows)
  • Professional: $49/month (2,000 tasks, unlimited Zaps)
  • Team & Company: Custom pricing for higher limits

Make Pricing (2024):

  • Free: 1,000 operations/month, 2 scenarios
  • Core: $9/month (10,000 operations, unlimited scenarios)
  • Pro: $16/month (15,000 operations, advanced features)
  • Teams & Enterprise: Custom plans with SLA support

Note: Make's "operations" count includes each module execution, while Zapier counts a "task" as one completed action. For multi-step workflows, Make can be more cost-effective.

Practical Use Cases

When to Choose Zapier

  • Quick integrations: You need to connect two apps in minutes (e.g., "Send new Typeform responses to Google Sheets").
  • Non-technical teams: Marketing, sales, or support teams with no coding experience.
  • Massive app library: You rely on niche or legacy apps that Make doesn't support.

Example: A small business owner uses Zapier to automatically create a Mailchimp subscriber when a WooCommerce order is placed, then send a thank-you SMS via Twilio.

When to Choose Make

  • Complex workflows: You need conditional logic, loops, or data aggregation (e.g., "Fetch orders from Shopify, calculate shipping costs, and update inventory in multiple systems").
  • Data-heavy tasks: You're processing large datasets with transformations.
  • Budget-conscious: You need high-volume automation at a lower cost.

Example: A SaaS startup uses Make to sync user data from Stripe to a custom CRM, filter active subscribers, and send personalized onboarding sequences via Intercom—all in one visual scenario.

Pros and Cons Summary

Zapier Pros

  • Easiest to learn and use
  • Largest app integration library
  • Excellent customer support and documentation
  • Reliable uptime and performance

Zapier Cons

  • Limited workflow complexity
  • Expensive for high-volume tasks
  • Basic data transformation capabilities
  • Fewer customization options for developers

Make Pros

  • Powerful visual workflow builder
  • Advanced data manipulation and logic
  • More affordable for high-volume usage
  • Excellent for technical users and developers

Make Cons

  • Steeper learning curve
  • Smaller app library (but growing)
  • Less intuitive for non-technical users
  • Occasional performance issues with complex scenarios

Actionable Tips for Choosing

  • Start with your skill level: If you're new to automation, begin with Zapier's free tier. As your needs grow, evaluate Make for more complex workflows.
  • Test both platforms: Both offer free tiers. Create a simple workflow (e.g., "Save email attachments to Dropbox") in each to compare usability.
  • Consider your budget: Calculate your monthly task/operation volume. For under 10,000 tasks, Zapier may be fine. For 50,000+ tasks, Make is likely more cost-effective.
  • Leverage hybrid approaches: Some teams use Zapier for simple integrations and Make for complex data pipelines. You can even connect them via webhooks.
  • Check app availability: Before committing, verify that your critical apps are supported. Zapier's directory is searchable; Make's can be explored on their website.

Conclusion

Both Zapier and Make are powerful automation tools, but they serve different audiences. Zapier is the best choice for simplicity, speed, and breadth of integrations—perfect for small businesses, marketers, and non-technical users. Make is the superior option for complex, data-driven workflows
