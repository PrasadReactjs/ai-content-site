Slack vs Microsoft Teams: The Ultimate Comparison for 2025

When it comes to team collaboration software, two names dominate the conversation: Slack and Microsoft Teams. Both platforms have passionate user bases and robust feature sets, but choosing between them can feel overwhelming. If you’re searching for a Slack alternative or evaluating which tool best fits your organization, this detailed comparison will help you make an informed decision. We’ll break down pricing, features, integrations, user experience, and security to highlight where each platform excels—and where it falls short.

Overview: What Are Slack and Microsoft Teams?

Slack launched in 2013 and quickly became synonymous with workplace messaging. Its intuitive interface, powerful search, and extensive third-party integrations made it a favorite among startups, tech companies, and remote teams. Slack is built around channels—organized conversations by topic, project, or team—and offers a clean, user-friendly experience.

Microsoft Teams arrived in 2017 as part of the Office 365 ecosystem. It integrates deeply with Microsoft’s suite of productivity tools (Word, Excel, SharePoint, Outlook) and is designed for organizations already invested in Microsoft’s ecosystem. Teams combines chat, video conferencing, file storage, and app integration into a single hub, making it a natural choice for enterprises.

Pricing and Plans

Slack Pricing

  • Free Plan: 10,000 message history, 10 app integrations, 1:1 video calls, and 5GB storage.
  • Pro Plan ($8.75/user/month): Unlimited message history, unlimited integrations, group video calls, 10GB storage per user, and guest access.
  • Business+ Plan ($15/user/month): Adds 99.99% uptime SLA, user provisioning, and 24/7 support.
  • Enterprise Grid (custom pricing): Unlimited workspaces, advanced compliance, and enterprise-grade security.

Microsoft Teams Pricing

  • Free Plan: Unlimited chat, 10GB shared storage, 60-minute group calls, and 300 participants per meeting.
  • Microsoft Teams Essentials ($4/user/month): Adds 30-hour meeting duration, 10GB storage per user, and admin controls.
  • Microsoft 365 Business Basic ($6/user/month): Includes Teams, web versions of Office apps, 1TB storage, and email hosting.
  • Microsoft 365 Business Standard ($12.50/user/month): Adds desktop Office apps and advanced meeting features.

Key Takeaway: Microsoft Teams is generally more affordable, especially for organizations already using Microsoft 365. Slack’s pricing is higher per user, but its free tier offers more generous message history and integration limits.

User Interface and Ease of Use

Slack is renowned for its polished, intuitive interface. The left sidebar displays channels and direct messages, and the search bar (powered by a powerful search engine) lets you find messages, files, and people instantly. Slack’s design feels modern and lightweight, with minimal clutter. New users often find it easier to adopt because it mimics familiar social media patterns.

Microsoft Teams has a more complex interface, largely due to its integration with the entire Microsoft ecosystem. The left rail includes Activity, Chat, Teams, Calendar, Calls, and Files. While this provides deep functionality, it can feel overwhelming for new users. Teams also uses a "tab" system within channels, which can be powerful but requires more clicks to navigate.

Winner: Slack for simplicity and onboarding speed. Teams for depth, if you’re willing to invest time in learning.

Communication Features

Messaging and Channels

Both platforms support channels, direct messages, and group conversations. Slack’s threading is cleaner—replies stay in a separate thread, reducing noise. Teams also supports threads, but they can sometimes feel buried in the main conversation. Slack’s "huddle" feature allows spontaneous voice calls, while Teams offers "walkie-talkie" functionality for frontline workers.

Video and Voice Calls

Slack supports 1:1 video calls on the free plan and group calls (up to 50 participants) on paid plans. However, Slack’s video capabilities are basic compared to Teams. Microsoft Teams offers enterprise-grade video conferencing with up to 1,000 participants (or 10,000 with live events), background blur, breakout rooms, and recording. Teams also integrates with Microsoft’s Phone System for VoIP calling.

Winner: Microsoft Teams for advanced video and voice features. Slack is sufficient for smaller teams with simpler needs.

Integrations and App Ecosystem

Slack has over 2,400 apps in its directory, including deep integrations with Google Drive, Asana, Trello, Zoom, and Salesforce. Slack’s API is developer-friendly, allowing custom bots and workflows. The platform also supports "Workflow Builder" for no-code automation.

Microsoft Teams integrates natively with Office 365 apps (Word, Excel, SharePoint, OneNote, Power BI) and offers over 1,400 third-party apps. However, many integrations require additional configuration or are limited to paid plans. Teams’ native integration with SharePoint and OneDrive makes file sharing seamless for Microsoft shops.

Winner: Slack for breadth and ease of third-party integration. Microsoft Teams for deep integration with Microsoft’s own tools.

Security and Compliance

Both platforms offer robust security features, but they differ in focus. Slack provides enterprise-grade security with SOC 2, GDPR compliance, HIPAA (on Business+ and Enterprise Grid), and data encryption at rest and in transit. Slack also supports enterprise mobility management (EMM) and data loss prevention (DLP).

Microsoft Teams benefits from Microsoft’s extensive security infrastructure, including Azure Active Directory, multi-factor authentication (MFA), conditional access, and advanced threat protection. Teams is compliant with a wide range of standards, including HIPAA, FedRAMP, and ISO 27001. For organizations in regulated industries, Teams often has the edge due to Microsoft’s long history with enterprise compliance.

Winner: Microsoft Teams for breadth of compliance certifications and enterprise security features. Slack is still excellent but may require higher-tier plans for full compliance.

File Storage and Sharing

Slack stores files within its own cloud (10GB per user on Pro, 20GB on Business+). You can also connect Google Drive, Dropbox, or Box. Slack’s search makes finding files easy, but storage limits can be restrictive for large teams.

Microsoft Teams uses SharePoint and OneDrive for file storage, offering 1TB per organization (plus 10GB per user on Essentials). This means near-unlimited storage for most teams, with version history and co-authoring in Office apps. Teams’ file management is more powerful but can be complex to navigate.

Winner: Microsoft Teams for storage capacity and Office integration. Slack for simplicity and third-party cloud support.

Practical Tips for Choosing

  • Start with your existing ecosystem. If your company uses Microsoft 365, Teams is the natural choice. If you rely on Google Workspace, Slack integrates better.
  • Consider team size and culture. Small, agile teams often prefer Slack’s speed and simplicity. Large enterprises with complex compliance needs may lean toward Teams.
  • Evaluate your budget. Teams is cheaper per user, but Slack’s free tier is more generous for small teams testing the waters.
  • Test both with a pilot group. Run a 30-day trial with a representative team. Pay attention to how users adopt the platform and whether they find it intuitive.
  • Think about integrations. List your must-have apps (e.g., CRM, project management, HR tools) and check which platform offers native or easy connections.
  • Plan for growth. If you anticipate scaling quickly, consider which platform’s pricing and features will accommodate your future needs.

Conclusion: Which Should You Choose?

There’s no one-size-fits-all answer. Slack excels in user experience, third-party integrations, and developer flexibility. It’s ideal for tech-forward organizations, startups, and teams that prioritize speed and simplicity.
