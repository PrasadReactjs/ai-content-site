Hotjar vs FullStory: A Detailed Comparison for UX Optimization

In the competitive world of user experience (UX) research and conversion rate optimization, choosing the right analytics tool can make or break your strategy. Two of the most popular platforms—Hotjar and FullStory—offer powerful insights into user behavior, but they cater to different needs and budgets. This comprehensive comparison will help you decide which tool aligns best with your goals, with a special focus on Hotjar’s strengths.

Overview: What Are Hotjar and FullStory?

Hotjar is an all-in-one behavior analytics and feedback platform that combines session recordings, heatmaps, surveys, and form analysis. It’s designed to be accessible for startups, small businesses, and marketers who need quick, actionable insights without a steep learning curve. Hotjar emphasizes simplicity and affordability, making it a favorite for teams that want to “see” what users do and hear what they say.

FullStory, on the other hand, is a more enterprise-focused tool that specializes in session replay and advanced analytics. It uses machine learning to automatically surface key events (like rage clicks or dead clicks) and offers robust search capabilities for large datasets. FullStory is often chosen by product teams at scale who need deep technical analysis.

Key Features Comparison

1. Session Recordings

Both tools offer session recordings, but the approach differs:

  • Hotjar: Records user sessions with a focus on clarity and ease of use. You can filter recordings by page, device, or user behavior (e.g., users who clicked a specific button). Hotjar also offers a “Watch Now” feature to see live sessions in real time—perfect for debugging or immediate feedback.
  • FullStory: Provides “session replay” with advanced search via its DevTools-like console. You can find sessions based on technical events (e.g., JavaScript errors) and use “Rage Click” or “Dead Click” auto-detection. FullStory’s recordings are more data-heavy but can be overwhelming for non-technical users.

2. Heatmaps

Heatmaps are a standout feature for Hotjar:

  • Hotjar: Offers click, move, and scroll heatmaps that are easy to set up with a single snippet of code. You can view heatmaps for specific pages, devices, or time periods. The visual overlay is intuitive—you see exactly where users click, hover, or stop scrolling.
  • FullStory: Heatmaps are available but less prominent. FullStory focuses more on session-level analysis, and its heatmaps require more configuration. For teams that prioritize heatmaps, Hotjar is the clear winner.

3. Feedback Tools (Surveys & Polls)

This is an area where Hotjar shines:

  • Hotjar: Includes built-in feedback widgets (on-site surveys, polls, and incoming feedback). You can trigger a survey after a specific action (e.g., “Was this page helpful?”) or collect open-ended feedback. This qualitative data complements quantitative analytics perfectly.
  • FullStory: Does not offer native feedback tools. You would need to integrate with a third-party survey tool (like SurveyMonkey or Typeform) to capture user opinions. This adds complexity and cost.

4. Form Analysis

Both tools help you understand form friction, but Hotjar is more straightforward:

  • Hotjar: Its form analysis feature identifies which fields users struggle with (e.g., repeated clicks, long pauses, or abandonment). You can see drop-off rates at each field and even view session recordings of users who abandoned the form.
  • FullStory: Offers form analytics through its session search capabilities, but it’s less visual. You’ll need to manually set up events to track field interactions.

5. Pricing & Scalability

Pricing is a major differentiator:

  • Hotjar: Offers a generous free plan (up to 35 daily sessions) and paid plans starting at $39/month (Plus) and $99/month (Business). The free tier includes heatmaps, recordings, and surveys—ideal for small teams or solo entrepreneurs.
  • FullStory: Starts with a limited free plan (1,000 sessions/month) but its paid plans begin at $200/month (Business) and scale quickly. Enterprise pricing can exceed $1,000/month. FullStory’s cost makes it prohibitive for many small businesses.

Practical Tips: When to Choose Hotjar

Based on the comparison, here are actionable scenarios where Hotjar is the better choice:

  • You need quick, visual insights: If your goal is to see where users click or scroll on a landing page, Hotjar heatmaps are ready in minutes. No complex setup required.
  • You want to combine behavior with feedback: Use Hotjar to run a survey alongside a session recording. For example, after a user abandons a checkout page, show a survey asking “What stopped you from completing the purchase?” This dual approach is seamless in Hotjar.
  • You’re on a tight budget: The free plan of Hotjar is powerful enough for most small to medium websites. You can start with recordings and heatmaps without spending a dime.
  • You prioritize ease of use: Hotjar’s interface is designed for marketers, designers, and product managers—not just developers. You can share reports with stakeholders without technical explanations.

When FullStory Might Be Better

FullStory excels in specific enterprise scenarios:

  • You need to search across millions of sessions for a specific user event (e.g., “find all sessions where a user clicked ‘Add to Cart’ and then encountered a 404 error”).
  • Your team has dedicated engineers who can set up custom events and integrate FullStory with your product development workflow.
  • You require advanced privacy controls (e.g., masking specific HTML elements by default).

However, for most businesses, Hotjar offers 80% of FullStory’s functionality at 20% of the cost.

Conclusion: Which Tool Should You Choose?

After this detailed comparison, the verdict is clear: Hotjar is the superior choice for teams that value simplicity, affordability, and a complete suite of UX tools (recordings, heatmaps, and feedback) in one platform. It allows you to quickly identify usability issues, gather user opinions, and iterate on your website—all without draining your budget or requiring extensive technical training.

FullStory remains a powerful option for large enterprises with complex technical requirements, but its high cost and steep learning curve make it overkill for most small to mid-sized projects.
