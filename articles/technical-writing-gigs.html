How to Screen and Bid on Technical Writing Gigs: A Freelancer's Guide to Winning Work

Technical writing is one of the most rewarding freelance careers, offering consistent demand, competitive pay, and the chance to work with cutting-edge technology. However, landing the right technical writing gigs requires more than just strong writing skills—you need to know how to screen opportunities effectively and craft bids that stand out. In this guide, we'll walk you through every step of the process, from identifying quality freelance opportunities to writing proposals that win clients.

Why Technical Writing Gigs Are Worth Pursuing

Before diving into the bidding process, it's important to understand the value of technical writing gigs. Companies across industries—from software development to medical devices—need clear documentation for APIs, user manuals, help centers, and compliance guides. According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, technical writing jobs are projected to grow faster than average, and freelance technical writers can earn between $40 and $100+ per hour depending on specialization.

The key to success lies in targeting the right opportunities. Not all freelance listings are created equal, and screening them carefully saves you time and frustration.

How to Screen Technical Writing Freelance Opportunities

Effective screening is your first line of defense against low-paying, poorly scoped, or scam projects. Here's a systematic approach to evaluating each listing.

1. Evaluate the Project Description

A well-written job post should include:

  • Clear scope: What type of document is needed (API docs, user guides, white papers)?
  • Target audience: Who will read the document (developers, end users, executives)?
  • Deliverables: Specific outputs (e.g., 10 pages, 5 tutorials, one style guide).
  • Tools required: Common platforms like MadCap Flare, Adobe FrameMaker, or Markdown editors.
  • Subject matter expertise: Preferred background (e.g., SaaS, healthcare, fintech).

Red flags: Vague descriptions like "write tech stuff" or "need documentation ASAP" often indicate unclear expectations. Avoid listings that ask for free samples or don't mention a budget.

2. Research the Client

Before bidding, spend 10–15 minutes vetting the client:

  • Check their website: Do they have an existing product? Is it well-established or a startup?
  • Read reviews: On platforms like Upwork or Freelancer, look at client feedback from past freelancers.
  • Assess communication style: Professional clients respond to questions promptly and provide detailed answers.
  • Verify payment history: On platforms, check if the client has a verified payment method or a history of paying on time.

Pro tip: If the client is unresponsive during the screening phase, it's a warning sign for the project itself.

3. Assess the Budget and Timeline

Technical writing gigs vary widely in pay. To determine if a listing is worthwhile:

  • Calculate your minimum rate: Factor in research time, revisions, and overhead (e.g., software costs, taxes).
  • Compare to market rates: Entry-level projects might pay $20–$40/hour, while specialized work (e.g., API documentation for AI tools) can exceed $100/hour.
  • Evaluate the timeline: Unrealistic deadlines (e.g., "100 pages in 3 days") suggest poor planning or scope creep.

Actionable tip: Create a spreadsheet to track gigs you screen, noting budget, timeline, and red flags. This helps you identify patterns over time.

Crafting a Winning Bid for Technical Writing Gigs

Once you've identified a promising opportunity, your bid is your chance to shine. Follow these steps to write a proposal that converts.

1. Personalize Your Introduction

Generic bids get ignored. Start by referencing the client's project specifically:

"I read your post about needing a technical writer for your cloud-based inventory management API. With five years of experience documenting RESTful APIs, I can help your development team and end users understand your endpoints clearly."

This shows you've read the listing and understand their needs.

2. Demonstrate Relevant Expertise

Highlight your experience with similar projects:

  • Mention specific tools: If you're proficient in Swagger, Postman, or Git, say so.
  • Show portfolio samples: Link to 2–3 examples of technical writing gigs you've completed, ideally in the same industry or document type.
  • Explain your process: Describe how you gather information (e.g., interviewing SMEs, testing software) and ensure accuracy.

Example: "For a recent client, I created a 50-page API reference guide using Markdown and Docusaurus, reducing their support tickets by 30%."

3. Address the Client's Pain Points

Think about the challenges the client likely faces:

  • Lack of time: Emphasize your ability to work independently and meet deadlines.
  • Inconsistent quality: Mention your editing and review process (e.g., peer reviews, style guide adherence).
  • Knowledge gaps: Offer to conduct a quick discovery call to clarify requirements.

Actionable advice: Include a brief "value proposition" in your bid—what unique benefit do you bring? For example, "I specialize in translating complex technical concepts into plain language for non-technical users."

4. Provide a Clear, Competitive Quote

Your pricing should reflect the project's complexity and your expertise. Options include:

  • Fixed price: Best for well-defined projects (e.g., "5 tutorial videos at $200 each").
  • Hourly rate: Suitable for ongoing work or projects with uncertain scope.
  • Milestone payments: Break the project into phases (e.g., outline, first draft, final revision) to reduce risk for both parties.

Pro tip: If the client's budget is lower than your rate, explain what you can deliver within their budget (e.g., a shorter document or fewer revisions). This shows flexibility without undervaluing your work.

5. Include a Call to Action

End your bid with a clear next step:

"I'd love to discuss your project further. Please let me know a convenient time for a quick 15-minute call to clarify your requirements. I'm available to start next week."

This invites collaboration and moves the conversation forward.

Common Mistakes to Avoid When Bidding

Even experienced freelancers sometimes fall into these traps:

  • Overpromising: Don't claim expertise you don't have. If you're new to a tool, say you're a fast learner.
  • Ignoring instructions: If the listing asks for a specific subject line or portfolio format, follow it exactly.
  • Being too generic: "I'm a great writer" says nothing. Instead, say, "I've written 20+ API docs for SaaS companies."
  • Neglecting grammar and formatting: Your bid should be error-free and well-structured—it's a sample of your work.

Tools and Resources for Technical Writing Freelancers

To streamline your screening and bidding process, use these resources:

  • Freelance platforms: Upwork, Freelancer, and Toptal (for higher-end gigs).
  • Job boards: ProBlogger, WriteJobs, and LinkedIn's "Freelance" filter.
  • Portfolio sites: Contently, Journo Portfolio, or a personal website with samples.
  • Rate guides: The Editorial Freelancers Association publishes annual rate charts for technical writing.
  • Time tracking: Toggl or Harvest to log hours for hourly projects.
