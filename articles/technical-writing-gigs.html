How to Screen and Bid on Technical Writing Gigs: A Freelancer’s Guide to Winning Work

Technical writing is one of the most rewarding and in-demand freelance niches. Companies need clear, user-friendly documentation for software, hardware, APIs, and internal processes—and they’re willing to pay skilled writers handsomely for it. But with thousands of freelancers competing for the same projects, simply finding a “technical writing gigs” listing isn’t enough. You need to know how to screen opportunities effectively and craft bids that stand out.

In this guide, I’ll walk you through a proven system for evaluating freelance technical writing opportunities and writing proposals that get you hired. Whether you’re new to the field or a seasoned pro looking to refine your approach, these strategies will help you land more projects—and better-paying ones.

Part 1: Screening Technical Writing Gigs—Don’t Apply to Everything

One of the biggest mistakes freelancers make is applying to every “technical writing gigs” post they see. This wastes time and energy. Instead, use a screening framework to evaluate each opportunity before you invest effort in a bid.

1.1 Evaluate the Project Scope

Read the job description carefully. Look for specifics about:

  • Deliverables: Are they asking for a user manual, API documentation, a white paper, or something else? Make sure it matches your expertise.
  • Volume: How many pages, words, or topics? A 10-page quick start guide is very different from a 200-page software manual.
  • Timeline: Is the deadline realistic? Rushed projects often lead to burnout or poor quality work.
  • Tools required: Do they need MadCap Flare, Adobe FrameMaker, Markdown, or just Microsoft Word? If you don’t know the tool, factor in learning time.

1.2 Check the Client’s Reputation

On platforms like Upwork, Freelancer, or LinkedIn, look at the client’s history:

  • How many projects have they posted?
  • What’s their average rating from previous freelancers?
  • Do they have a clear payment history (verified payment, milestones used)?
  • Have they hired technical writers before? If so, read the feedback left for those writers.

A client with a history of vague requirements or low ratings is a red flag. Move on.

1.3 Assess the Budget Realistically

Technical writing is a specialized skill. If the budget is $50 for a 50-page manual, that’s a red flag. Use industry benchmarks:

  • Beginner technical writers: $25–$50 per hour
  • Intermediate: $50–$100 per hour
  • Expert (API docs, medical/legal): $100–$200+ per hour

If the budget is fixed, calculate the hourly rate based on your estimated hours. If it’s below your minimum, politely decline or negotiate.

1.4 Look for Clarity in Requirements

Well-written job posts usually indicate a professional client who understands the process. Red flags include:

  • Vague descriptions like “Write some technical stuff”
  • No mention of subject matter or audience
  • Requests for free samples or unpaid test assignments

If the post is unclear, you can ask clarifying questions—but if the client can’t articulate what they need, the project is likely to be problematic.

Part 2: Crafting a Winning Bid for Technical Writing Gigs

Once you’ve screened a gig and decided it’s a good fit, your bid is your chance to shine. Generic proposals get ignored. Here’s how to write one that gets noticed.

2.1 Personalize Your Opening

Never start with “Dear Sir/Madam” or “I’m interested in your project.” Instead, reference something specific from the job post:

“I read that you need documentation for your new SaaS platform’s REST API. I’ve written API docs for three similar projects, including one for a payment gateway integration.”

This immediately shows you’ve read the post and understand the context.

2.2 Showcase Relevant Experience

Don’t list every job you’ve ever done. Instead, highlight 2–3 projects that are directly relevant to this technical writing gig. For each, mention:

  • The type of documentation (user guide, API reference, etc.)
  • The tools you used
  • The outcome (e.g., “Reduced support tickets by 30% after I rewrote the installation guide”)

If you’re new to technical writing, you can still show relevant skills: experience with technical subject matter, strong research abilities, or familiarity with documentation tools.

2.3 Address the Client’s Pain Points

Many clients post technical writing gigs because they’re overwhelmed or their current documentation is confusing. Show empathy:

“I understand that unclear documentation can frustrate users and increase support costs. My approach focuses on clarity and usability—I test my drafts with real users to ensure they can follow the instructions.”

This demonstrates that you’re not just a writer, but a problem-solver.

2.4 Provide a Clear Process

Clients appreciate knowing how you’ll work. Outline your typical process for a technical writing project:

  • Research phase: Review existing materials, interview subject matter experts, and test the product.
  • Drafting: Write in clear, concise language following a style guide (or create one if needed).
  • Review & revision: Incorporate feedback from the client and SME reviews.
  • Final delivery: Provide the documentation in the required format (PDF, HTML, Markdown, etc.).

This shows you’re organized and professional.

2.5 Include a Relevant Sample

If the platform allows attachments or links, include a sample that’s similar to the project. If you don’t have a public sample, offer to write a short, relevant paragraph on a topic related to the gig. For example, if the gig is about a mobile app, write a sample help topic for a common task like “How to reset your password.”

2.6 Set Expectations for Communication

Technical writing often requires ongoing collaboration. Mention your availability:

“I’m available for daily check-ins via email or Slack. I can also schedule calls with your team to clarify technical details.”

This builds trust and shows you’re easy to work with.

2.7 Close with a Call to Action

End your bid by inviting the client to take the next step:

“I’d love to discuss your project in more detail. Would you be available for a quick 15-minute call this week? I can also provide a custom timeline and quote once I understand the full scope.”

A specific, low-friction call to action increases the chance of a response.

Part 3: Common Mistakes to Avoid When Bidding on Technical Writing Gigs

Even experienced writers make these errors. Avoid them to improve your win rate.

3.1 Overpromising on Expertise

If you’re not an expert in the subject matter (e.g., quantum computing or medical devices), be honest. You can offer to learn, but don’t claim expertise you don’t have. Clients will discover the truth during the project, and it will damage your reputation.

3.2 Writing a Generic Proposal

Copy-pasting the same bid for every gig is a fast track to rejection. Each proposal should feel custom-written for that specific client and project.

3.3 Underpricing Your Work

Many freelancers think a low price will win them jobs. In technical writing, low prices often signal low quality. Clients who value good documentation are willing to pay for it. Charge what you’re worth.

3.4 Ignoring the Client’s Instructions

Some job posts ask you to include a specific phrase (e.g., “Include the word ‘blueprint’ in your response”) to filter out applicants who don’t read carefully. Always follow instructions exactly.
