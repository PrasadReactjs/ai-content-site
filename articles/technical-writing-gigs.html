How to Screen and Bid on Technical Writing Gigs: A Freelancer’s Guide to Winning Work

If you’ve landed on this page searching for technical writing gigs, you’re likely ready to turn your knack for clear communication into a thriving freelance career. The freelance marketplace is brimming with opportunities—from documenting APIs for startups to writing user manuals for enterprise software. But the difference between a gig that pays peanuts and one that builds your portfolio lies in how you screen and bid on each project.

In this guide, we’ll walk you through a proven framework for evaluating technical writing gigs, crafting winning proposals, and landing high-quality freelance work. Whether you’re new to the field or an experienced writer looking to refine your approach, these actionable steps will help you stand out in a competitive market.

1. Start with the Right Mindset: Screening Before Bidding

Before you type a single word of your proposal, you need to decide if the gig is worth your time. Many freelancers make the mistake of bidding on every technical writing gigs they see, only to end up with low-paying projects or clients who don’t respect their expertise. Screening is your first line of defense.

Key Factors to Screen For

  • Budget and scope: Look for gigs that clearly state a budget range. If the budget is too low (e.g., $50 for a 5,000-word manual), it’s a red flag. A realistic starting point for entry-level technical writing is $30–$50 per hour, while experienced writers can command $75–$150 per hour.
  • Project clarity: Does the client describe the deliverable in detail? Vague postings like “Need help with documentation” often lead to scope creep. Favor clients who specify the type of document (e.g., API reference, user guide, release notes) and desired format (PDF, HTML, Markdown).
  • Client credibility: Check the client’s history on platforms like Upwork or Freelancer. Look for verified payment methods, past reviews, and a completed projects percentage above 90%.
  • Topic familiarity: While you can learn on the job, it’s smarter to bid on topics you already understand. If the gig involves medical device documentation and you have no healthcare background, consider passing unless you’re willing to research heavily.

Create a simple checklist for each potential gig. If it fails on two or more points, skip it. Your time is better spent on well-defined, fairly compensated technical writing gigs.

2. Research the Client and the Project Deeply

Once a gig passes your initial screen, dive deeper. This step separates average freelancers from professionals who win repeat work.

What to Research

  • The company: Visit their website. Read their blog, product pages, and existing documentation. Note their tone—is it formal, conversational, or developer-focused? Your proposal should mirror this tone.
  • The competition: If the gig is for a popular product (e.g., a new SaaS tool), look at how competitors document similar features. Identify gaps you can fill.
  • The target audience: Who will read the documentation? Beginners, power users, or system administrators? Tailor your bid to show you understand their needs.
  • The tools involved: Does the client mention specific tools like MadCap Flare, Adobe FrameMaker, or Git-based workflows? If you’re proficient, highlight it. If not, be honest about your willingness to learn.

This research not only helps you write a better proposal but also lets you ask smart questions during the interview. For example, “I noticed your current API docs use Swagger. Would you like me to contribute to that format or move to a different tool like ReadMe?”

3. Craft a Winning Proposal for Technical Writing Gigs

Your proposal is your sales pitch. It must demonstrate that you understand the client’s problem and can solve it efficiently. Generic proposals rarely win technical writing gigs—you need to personalize every submission.

Structure Your Proposal

  • Subject line: Make it specific. Instead of “Proposal for technical writing gig,” use “Experienced API writer for your SaaS documentation project.”
  • Opening: Show you’ve done your homework. Example: “I read your job post for documenting the new inventory module. I’ve worked with similar ERP systems and understand the need for clear, step-by-step instructions for warehouse managers.”
  • Your relevant experience: Highlight 1–2 past projects that are similar in scope or industry. Use metrics if possible: “I wrote a 200-page user guide for a fintech app that reduced support tickets by 30%.”
  • Your process: Explain how you’ll approach the project. For instance: “I’ll start by interviewing your subject-matter experts, then create an outline for your review, write the first draft, and revise based on your feedback. I use Markdown and GitHub for version control.”
  • Sample or portfolio link: Attach a relevant sample or link to your online portfolio. If you don’t have a direct sample, offer to write a short trial piece (e.g., 200 words on a feature of their product).
  • Pricing and timeline: Be transparent. Instead of a vague range, offer a fixed price or hourly rate based on your research. Example: “Based on the scope you described, I estimate this will take 40 hours at $50/hour, or a fixed price of $2,000.”

Common Proposal Mistakes to Avoid

  • Writing a novel—keep it under 500 words unless the client asks for more.
  • Using jargon that doesn’t match the client’s industry.
  • Neglecting to proofread—technical writing gigs demand precision.
  • Undercutting your rate to win the bid. This often leads to resentment and low-quality work.

4. Practical Tips for Bidding on Different Types of Technical Writing Gigs

Not all technical writing gigs are created equal. Here’s how to tailor your approach for common categories:

API Documentation

Focus on your ability to understand code samples, endpoints, and authentication flows. Mention experience with tools like Postman, Swagger, or Stoplight. In your bid, offer to improve an existing endpoint reference or write a getting-started tutorial.

User Manuals and Guides

Emphasize your ability to write for non-technical audiences. Share examples of how you’ve simplified complex processes. Offer to create a table of contents or a sample page as part of your proposal.

Internal Documentation

These gigs often require confidentiality and adherence to company style guides. Highlight your reliability and experience working with subject-matter experts. Mention that you can work within their existing knowledge base (e.g., Confluence, Notion).

White Papers and Case Studies

These are more marketing-focused but still require technical accuracy. Showcase your research skills and ability to interview engineers or product managers. Include a sample of a previously published white paper.

5. How to Handle the Interview and Negotiation

If your proposal gets a response, congratulations! The next step is usually a video call or chat interview. Here’s how to prepare:

  • Ask clarifying questions: “Who is the primary audience for this document? What’s the biggest pain point you’re trying to solve?”
  • Discuss deliverables and milestones: Agree on a clear timeline with checkpoints. For example: “I’ll deliver an outline by day 3, first draft by day 10, and final version by day 20.”
  • Negotiate scope: If the client asks for extra features (like video scripts or screenshots), discuss how they affect the budget. Always get scope changes in writing.
  • Set communication expectations: How often will you check in? Weekly emails? Slack messages? Clear communication prevents misunderstandings.

When negotiating rates, remember that technical writing gigs are a specialized skill. Don’t be afraid to justify your rate with your experience and the value you bring. A well-written manual can save a company thousands in support costs.
