Ghost vs Substack: The Ultimate Comparison for Publishers in 2024

If you're a writer, blogger, or content creator looking to launch a newsletter or membership site, you've likely encountered two major platforms: Ghost and Substack. Both offer powerful tools to publish content, grow an audience, and monetize your work. But which one is right for you? In this detailed comparison, we'll break down every key aspect—from pricing and customization to monetization and ownership—to help you make an informed decision. By the end, you'll know exactly which platform aligns with your goals and how to get started.

Overview: What Are Ghost and Substack?

Ghost is an open-source content management system (CMS) designed specifically for modern publishing. It's a self-hosted or managed platform that gives you full control over your website, design, and data. Ghost is ideal for creators who want a professional, customizable site with built-in membership and subscription features.

Substack, on the other hand, is a hosted newsletter platform that prioritizes simplicity. It's a turnkey solution where you can start writing and sending emails immediately, with built-in paid subscriptions and a discovery network. Substack is perfect for writers who want to focus solely on content without worrying about technical setup.

Detailed Comparison

1. Ease of Use

Substack wins hands-down for simplicity. You sign up with an email, write a post, and hit publish. The editor is clean and minimal, with no distractions. There's no need to set up a domain, install plugins, or manage hosting. It's perfect if you want to start publishing within minutes.

Ghost requires a bit more setup. If you self-host, you'll need to install Ghost on a server (or use a managed service like Ghost(Pro)). The editor is also clean but offers more formatting options, embedding, and metadata controls. Once set up, it's intuitive, but the initial learning curve is steeper.

Practical Tip: If you're non-technical and want the fastest path to publishing, start with Substack. If you're willing to invest a few hours in setup for long-term flexibility, choose Ghost.

2. Customization and Design

Ghost is vastly superior here. You can choose from hundreds of free and premium themes, customize every CSS element, and even build a unique design from scratch. Ghost supports custom pages, navigation menus, and full control over your site's layout. This is critical if you want to build a brand, not just a newsletter.

Substack offers very limited design options. You can change your logo, colors, and header image, but the overall layout is fixed. All Substack publications look similar. While this simplicity can be appealing, it also means your site will look like every other Substack blog.

Actionable Advice: If branding and visual identity matter to you, Ghost is the clear choice. For example, creators like James Clear (author of Atomic Habits) use Ghost to build a distinct, professional website. Substack is better if you prioritize content over design.

3. Monetization Features

Both platforms allow you to charge for subscriptions, but they approach monetization differently.

Substack takes a 10% cut of all paid subscriptions (plus Stripe processing fees). You can offer free and paid tiers, but the platform's focus is on newsletters. Substack also has a "Recommendations" feature that helps cross-promote other writers, which can grow your audience organically.

Ghost charges no transaction fees (only Stripe fees, typically 2.9% + $0.30). You can sell memberships, subscriptions, and even one-time products like e-books or courses. Ghost also supports multiple pricing tiers, donation-based models, and ad integration. Plus, you can use Stripe or other payment gateways.

Example: If you earn $10,000/month in subscriptions, Substack takes $1,000. Ghost takes $0. Over a year, that's $12,000 in savings—enough to cover Ghost(Pro) hosting for years.

4. Ownership and Portability

This is a critical difference. Ghost is open-source, meaning you own everything—your content, your subscriber list, your design, and your data. You can export everything at any time and move to another platform without losing your audience.

Substack hosts your content on its platform. While you can export your subscriber list (as a CSV), your content lives on Substack's servers. If Substack changes its terms, raises fees, or shuts down, you could lose access to your work. This is a risk many serious creators consider.

Practical Tip: If you're building a long-term business, prioritize ownership. Ghost gives you an exit strategy. Substack locks you into its ecosystem.

5. SEO and Traffic Generation

Ghost offers built-in SEO tools, including meta tags, XML sitemaps, canonical URLs, and structured data. You can use custom domains (e.g., yourname.com) which boost search engine rankings. Ghost also supports AMP (Accelerated Mobile Pages) for faster loading on mobile devices.

Substack uses a subdomain (e.g., yourname.substack.com), which is less SEO-friendly. Custom domains are available but require a paid plan. Even with a custom domain, Substack's SEO capabilities are basic—you can't control meta descriptions or schema markup as deeply.

Actionable Advice: If you want to drive organic traffic from Google, Ghost is better. For example, a Ghost site with proper SEO can rank for long-tail keywords like "best productivity tips for writers," while a Substack site will struggle to appear in search results.

6. Community and Discovery

Substack has a built-in network effect. Readers can discover your publication through Substack's recommendation engine, which suggests newsletters based on subscribers' interests. This can be a powerful growth tool, especially for new writers.

Ghost has no discovery network. You're responsible for driving your own traffic through social media, SEO, or advertising. However, Ghost integrates with tools like Zapier, Mailchimp, and Google Analytics, so you can build your own marketing funnel.

Practical Tip: If you're starting from zero and want a built-in audience, Substack's network is valuable. If you already have a following or are willing to invest in marketing, Ghost gives you more control.
