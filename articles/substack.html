Ghost vs Substack: Which Platform Is Right for Your Newsletter in 2024?

If you're a writer, creator, or publisher looking to launch a newsletter and build a paid subscription business, you've likely narrowed your choices down to two leading platforms: Ghost and Substack. Both offer powerful tools for publishing, email marketing, and monetization, but they take fundamentally different approaches. This comprehensive comparison will help you decide which platform aligns best with your goals, technical skills, and growth ambitions.

Overview: Ghost vs Substack

What is Ghost?

Ghost is an open-source, fully customizable content management system (CMS) designed specifically for modern publishing. It combines a powerful editor, built-in membership features, and native email delivery. Ghost gives you complete ownership of your content and data, and you can host it yourself or use Ghost(Pro) for managed hosting.

What is Substack?

Substack is a hosted, all-in-one platform for newsletters and paid subscriptions. It's known for its simplicity—you can start publishing and charging for subscriptions in minutes with zero technical setup. Substack handles hosting, email delivery, and payment processing for you, but you give up some control and ownership in exchange for that convenience.

Key Comparison Areas

1. Ownership and Control

Ghost is the clear winner here. Because it's open-source, you own everything: your content, your subscriber list, your data, and your brand. You can export your entire site and subscriber database at any time. With Substack, your content lives on Substack's servers, and while you can export subscriber emails, you cannot easily migrate your full publication elsewhere. This lock-in is a major consideration for long-term growth.

2. Ease of Use

Substack is designed for absolute beginners. You sign up, write your first post, and hit publish. There's no hosting setup, no domain configuration, and no learning curve. Ghost requires more initial effort—you need to choose a hosting option, set up a domain, and configure your publication. Ghost's editor is powerful but has more features to learn. However, once set up, Ghost is intuitive for daily use.

3. Customization and Design

Ghost offers extensive customization. You can use pre-built themes, modify them with HTML/CSS, or build your own from scratch. You control the entire look and feel of your site, including navigation, layout, and branding. Substack has very limited design options—you can change colors, add a logo, and tweak a few fonts, but every Substack publication looks essentially the same. For branding-conscious creators, Ghost is the better choice.

4. Monetization Features

Both platforms support paid subscriptions, but they differ in fees and flexibility. Substack takes a 10% cut of all subscription revenue, plus Stripe payment processing fees (2.9% + $0.30 per transaction). Ghost charges no platform fee—you only pay Stripe's processing fees. Ghost also supports multiple paid tiers, one-time payments, and more flexible pricing models. Substack's fee structure is simpler but more expensive at scale.

5. Email Delivery and Analytics

Ghost uses Amazon SES or Mailgun for email delivery, which is reliable but requires some configuration. You get detailed analytics on open rates, click-through rates, and subscriber growth. Substack handles email delivery automatically and provides basic analytics. Substack's email infrastructure is robust, but Ghost offers more granular data and integration with third-party analytics tools.

6. Community and Discovery

Substack has a built-in discovery feature—readers can find your publication through Substack's network, recommendations, and the "Notes" social feed. This can drive initial subscribers. Ghost has no discovery network; you're responsible for driving all your own traffic through SEO, social media, and other channels. If you want organic discovery, Substack has an edge—but you're also competing with every other publication on the platform.

Practical Tips for Choosing

  • Choose Ghost if: You want full ownership, plan to scale beyond a simple newsletter, need advanced customization, or want to avoid platform fees. Ghost is ideal for businesses, media companies, and serious creators who treat their publication as a long-term asset.
  • Choose Substack if: You're a solo writer who wants to start immediately with zero technical hassle, you value built-in discovery, or you're testing a newsletter idea with minimal upfront investment. Substack is perfect for beginners and those who prioritize simplicity over control.
  • Hybrid approach: Some creators start on Substack to validate their idea, then migrate to Ghost once they outgrow Substack's limitations. This is possible but requires manual work—exporting subscriber emails and rebuilding your site.

SEO and Growth Potential

Ghost is built with SEO in mind. It generates clean, semantic HTML, supports meta tags, sitemaps, and structured data out of the box. You have full control over your URLs, canonical tags, and page speed optimization. This makes Ghost a powerful platform for driving organic traffic from search engines. Substack has basic SEO capabilities—your posts get indexed, but you have limited control over metadata and site structure. Substack's SEO is adequate but not competitive with a properly optimized Ghost site.

Actionable Advice for Your Decision

  1. Assess your technical comfort: If you're not comfortable with hosting, domains, and basic web setup, start with Substack. If you're willing to invest a weekend in setup, Ghost pays off long-term.
  2. Calculate your revenue break-even: At $1,000/month in subscription revenue, Substack takes $100 in platform fees. Ghost's hosting costs $9-$29/month. If you expect to earn more than a few hundred dollars monthly, Ghost becomes cheaper.
  3. Plan for growth: Ghost scales to thousands of subscribers and multiple team members seamlessly. Substack can handle growth too, but you'll be limited by its design and feature constraints.
  4. Test both: Sign up for Substack's free tier and Ghost's 14-day free trial. Write a few posts on each. See which workflow feels more natural for your writing process.

Conclusion: Which Should You Choose?

There's no single "best" platform—the right choice depends on your specific needs. Substack wins on simplicity and built-in discovery, making it ideal for writers who want to focus solely on writing without technical distractions. Ghost wins on ownership, customization, cost at scale, and SEO potential, making it the superior choice for serious publishers who treat their newsletter as a business.

Call to action: If you're ready to take control of your publishing future, start a free Ghost trial today at ghost.org. For a quick start with zero setup, sign up for Substack at substack.com. Whichever you choose, the most important step is to start writing and building your audience—the platform is just a tool.
