Ghost vs Substack: Which Platform is Right for Your Newsletter in 2024?

If you're a writer, creator, or publisher looking to launch a paid newsletter in 2024, you've likely encountered two major players: Ghost and Substack. Both platforms allow you to build an audience, send emails, and monetize your content through subscriptions. However, they take fundamentally different approaches to ownership, customization, and growth.

This detailed comparison will help you decide which platform aligns with your goals. We'll cover pricing, features, customization, SEO, monetization, and more—with a focus on helping you choose the right tool for your Substack-style publishing needs.

Overview: Ghost vs Substack at a Glance

Substack is a hosted platform designed for simplicity. You sign up, start writing, and can begin charging for subscriptions within minutes. It's a fully managed service—Substack handles hosting, security, and email delivery for you. In exchange, they take a 10% cut of your subscription revenue.

Ghost is an open-source publishing platform that gives you complete control. You can use Ghost's managed hosting (Ghost Pro) or self-host on your own server. Ghost charges a flat monthly fee (starting at $9/month for managed hosting) and takes 0% of your revenue. It's more powerful but requires more setup and technical know-how.

Let's break down the key differences in detail.

1. Pricing and Revenue Share

Substack Pricing

  • Free to start – No upfront costs for basic publishing.
  • 10% transaction fee – Substack takes 10% of all subscription revenue (on top of Stripe payment processing fees, typically 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction).
  • No hosting fees – Everything is included in the 10% cut.

Ghost Pricing

  • Ghost Pro hosting – Starts at $9/month for up to 500 members, $25/month for up to 1,000 members, and scales up.
  • 0% revenue share – Ghost takes no cut of your subscription income. You only pay Stripe processing fees.
  • Self-hosting – Free software, but you pay for server hosting (e.g., DigitalOcean, AWS) and maintenance.

Practical tip: If you plan to earn over $1,000/month from subscriptions, Ghost is almost always cheaper in the long run. Substack's 10% fee adds up quickly. For example, at $5,000/month in revenue, Substack takes $500, while Ghost Pro costs $25–$50/month.

2. Customization and Design Control

Substack: Limited but Clean

Substack offers a minimal, clean design that works out of the box. You can customize your publication's logo, colors, and a few layout options, but you cannot modify the HTML, CSS, or JavaScript. Your site will look very similar to every other Substack publication.

Ghost: Full Design Freedom

Ghost gives you complete control over your site's appearance. You can:

  • Use or create custom themes (Handlebars-based templates)
  • Edit CSS and HTML directly
  • Add custom pages, navigation menus, and footers
  • Integrate with third-party tools via Zapier, APIs, or custom code

Actionable advice: If branding and a unique look are important for your business (e.g., you're building a media brand or online course platform), Ghost is the better choice. If you just want to write and don't care about design, Substack's simplicity is a plus.

3. SEO and Content Ownership

This is one of the most critical differences for long-term growth.

Substack: Limited SEO and No Data Portability

  • Substack publications are hosted on a subdomain (e.g., yourname.substack.com). Google treats subdomains differently from custom domains, which can hurt SEO.
  • You cannot export your subscriber list or content easily. Substack owns the relationship with your readers.
  • No access to your site's code or server logs.

Ghost: Full SEO Control and Data Ownership

  • You can use a custom domain (e.g., yourname.com) which is better for SEO.
  • Ghost generates clean, semantic HTML and supports meta tags, sitemaps, and canonical URLs out of the box.
  • You own your content and subscriber data. You can export everything at any time.
  • Self-hosting gives you full control over your data and privacy.

Practical tip: If you're building a long-term business, owning your data and controlling your SEO is non-negotiable. Ghost gives you that; Substack does not.

4. Email Deliverability and Member Management

Substack: Built-in but Opaque

Substack handles email delivery automatically. They use their own infrastructure, which is generally reliable. However, you have limited control over deliverability settings, and you cannot see detailed analytics on bounces or spam complaints.

Ghost: Flexible Email Integration

Ghost allows you to connect your own email service provider (Mailgun, SendGrid, Amazon SES, etc.) or use Ghost's built-in email system (powered by Mailgun). This gives you:

  • Better control over deliverability and sender reputation.
  • Detailed email analytics (opens, clicks, bounces, unsubscribes).
  • Ability to segment your audience and send targeted campaigns.

Actionable advice: For serious email marketers, Ghost's flexibility is a major advantage. You can optimize deliverability and track performance more precisely.

5. Monetization Features

Substack

  • Paid subscriptions (monthly or yearly)
  • Free and paid tiers for content
  • Referral program (readers earn a cut of subscription fees for referrals)
  • No native support for one-time payments, memberships, or digital products

Ghost

  • Paid subscriptions (monthly, yearly, or custom intervals)
  • Free, paid, and premium tiers with member-only content
  • Native support for one-time payments (e.g., for digital downloads, courses)
  • Built-in membership management with Stripe integration
  • No referral program (but you can build one with third-party tools)

Key difference: Ghost's monetization is more flexible. You can sell individual articles, courses, or memberships alongside subscriptions. Substack is purely subscription-focused.

6. Community and Discovery Features

Substack has a built-in network effect. Readers can discover your publication through Substack's "Recommendations" feature, where other writers can recommend your newsletter to their audience. This can be a powerful growth driver for new writers.

Ghost has no such discovery network. Growth depends entirely on your own marketing efforts (SEO, social media, word of mouth, etc.).

Practical tip: If you're starting from zero and want to leverage existing audiences, Substack's network can help. However, this comes at the cost of owning your audience. Many successful writers eventually migrate to Ghost after building a following.

7. Ease of Use and Learning Curve

Substack: Extremely Easy

  • Sign up and start writing in minutes.
  • No technical setup required.
  • Simple editor with limited formatting options.

Ghost: Moderate Learning Curve

  • Requires setup (even Ghost Pro needs a few clicks to configure).
  • More powerful editor with markdown support, image galleries, and embeds.
  • You'll need to learn how to manage members, email settings, and themes.

Actionable advice: If you're non-technical and want to start publishing today, choose Substack. If you're willing to invest a few hours learning the platform, Ghost offers vastly more long-term value.
