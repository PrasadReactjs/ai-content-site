Mailchimp vs ConvertKit: Which Email Marketing Platform is Right for You in 2024?

Email marketing remains one of the most powerful tools for building relationships and driving sales. But with so many platforms available, choosing the right one can feel overwhelming. Two of the most popular contenders are Mailchimp and ConvertKit. While Mailchimp is a household name, ConvertKit has carved out a loyal following among creators, bloggers, and online educators. In this detailed comparison, we’ll break down the key differences, strengths, and weaknesses of each platform—with a special focus on why ConvertKit might be the game-changer your business needs.

Overview: Mailchimp vs ConvertKit

Mailchimp has been around since 2001 and serves millions of users worldwide. It’s known for its all-in-one marketing suite, including email campaigns, landing pages, social media ads, and CRM features. ConvertKit, launched in 2013, is a leaner platform built specifically for professional creators—think authors, course creators, podcasters, and bloggers—who need simplicity, automation, and audience segmentation.

Both platforms are excellent, but they cater to very different audiences. Let’s dive into the specifics.

1. Ease of Use and Onboarding

Mailchimp: Feature-Rich but Steep Learning Curve

Mailchimp offers a lot of features, but that comes at a cost: complexity. The interface has evolved over the years, but many users report feeling overwhelmed by the sheer number of options. Setting up automations can be confusing, and the drag-and-drop editor, while powerful, sometimes feels cluttered. For beginners, Mailchimp can be intimidating.

ConvertKit: Minimalist and Creator-Focused

ConvertKit takes a “less is more” approach. The interface is clean, intuitive, and purpose-built for email marketing. You can create a landing page, set up a simple automation, and start sending emails in minutes. The visual automation builder is a standout feature—it allows you to map out subscriber journeys with simple “if/then” logic. For creators who want to spend less time tinkering and more time connecting with their audience, ConvertKit is a dream.

Winner: ConvertKit – If you value simplicity and speed, ConvertKit wins hands-down.

2. Email Automation and Sequences

Mailchimp: Powerful but Rigid

Mailchimp offers robust automation capabilities, including triggered emails, abandoned cart sequences, and multi-step journeys. However, its automation logic is often described as “clunky.” You’ll need to navigate through multiple menus and settings to create a simple sequence. For advanced users, Mailchimp’s customer journey builder (available on higher-tier plans) is flexible, but it’s not beginner-friendly.

ConvertKit: Visual and Flexible

ConvertKit’s automation is its secret weapon. The visual editor lets you drag and drop triggers, conditions, and actions to create complex sequences effortlessly. You can tag subscribers based on their behavior, send different emails based on which link they clicked, and even set up “rules” to automate daily tasks. This flexibility is perfect for creators who run webinars, launch courses, or nurture leads over time.

Winner: ConvertKit – For ease of creating and managing automations, ConvertKit is superior.

3. Segmentation and Tagging

Mailchimp: List-Based with Limited Tags

Mailchimp uses a combination of lists, groups, and segments. You can create segments based on subscriber activity, but the system feels rigid. Tags exist but are limited in functionality compared to ConvertKit. For example, you cannot easily assign multiple tags to a subscriber without jumping through hoops. This makes advanced segmentation more difficult.

ConvertKit: Tag-Based System

ConvertKit is built around tags. Every subscriber can have unlimited tags, and you can use these tags to trigger automations, send targeted emails, and filter reports. This tag-based approach is incredibly powerful for creators who want to send highly personalized content. For instance, you can tag subscribers who opened a specific email, clicked a link, or purchased a product, then send them a follow-up sequence tailored to that action.

Winner: ConvertKit – If you rely on granular segmentation, ConvertKit is the clear choice.

4. Pricing: Which One Gives You More Value?

Mailchimp: Free Plan but Expensive Scaling

Mailchimp offers a generous free plan for up to 500 contacts, but it comes with limitations: Mailchimp branding, basic templates, and limited support. As your list grows, costs can skyrocket. The Standard plan (for 500 contacts) starts at around $13/month, but that price increases significantly as you add subscribers. Features like advanced segmentation and A/B testing are locked behind higher tiers.

ConvertKit: Free Plan for Up to 1,000 Subscribers

ConvertKit also offers a free plan for up to 1,000 subscribers, with no branding on your emails. The Creator plan (for 1,000 subscribers) costs $29/month, which includes all features—unlimited landing pages, automations, and integrations. While ConvertKit has no free plan beyond 1,000 subscribers, its pricing is predictable and scales linearly. For creators with 5,000–10,000 subscribers, ConvertKit often ends up being more affordable than Mailchimp.

Winner: Tie (depends on your needs) – Mailchimp wins for small, free users; ConvertKit wins for creators who need advanced features without hidden costs.

5. Templates and Design Flexibility

Mailchimp: Hundreds of Templates

Mailchimp offers a vast library of pre-designed email templates, plus a drag-and-drop editor. You can customize colors, fonts, and layouts to match your brand. However, the editor can be slow, and some templates are not fully responsive. If you need fancy designs, Mailchimp is a solid choice.

ConvertKit: Simple, Text-Focused Templates

ConvertKit takes a different approach: its templates are intentionally simple and text-heavy. This is because research shows that plain-text emails often have higher open and click rates. You can add images and embed buttons, but the focus is on content, not flashy design. If you’re a blogger or author who wants your words to shine, ConvertKit’s minimalist templates are a plus.

Winner: Mailchimp – If design variety matters to you, Mailchimp has more options. But for performance, ConvertKit’s simplicity often wins.

6. Integrations and Ecosystem

Mailchimp: Extensive but Costly

Mailchimp integrates with hundreds of apps, including Shopify, WooCommerce, WordPress, and Salesforce. However, many of these integrations are limited on lower-tier plans. For example, to integrate with Shopify, you may need the Standard plan or higher. This can be frustrating for small businesses on a budget.

ConvertKit: Curated for Creators

ConvertKit integrates with tools creators actually use: WordPress, Teachable, Gumroad, Stripe, Zapier, and more. The integrations are well-documented and easy to set up. While the list is smaller than Mailchimp’s, it’s more focused. If you sell digital products or run a membership site, ConvertKit’s integrations feel seamless.

Winner: Tie – Mailchimp has more integrations; ConvertKit has better integrations for creators.

7. Deliverability and Spam Compliance

Mailchimp: Good but Shared Sending Reputation

Mailchimp has decent deliverability rates, but because it hosts so many users (including spammers), your emails may occasionally land in spam folders. Mailchimp uses a shared IP pool by default, which can affect your sender reputation.

ConvertKit: Excellent Deliverability

ConvertKit prioritizes deliverability. The platform uses dedicated IP addresses for higher-tier plans and actively monitors sender reputation. Many creators report that their emails consistently land in the primary inbox, not promotions or spam. This is a huge advantage for building trust with your audience.

Winner: ConvertKit – If deliverability is a priority (and it should be), ConvertKit is the better choice.

Practical Tips and Actionable Advice

Here’s how to choose between Mailchimp and ConvertKit:

  • Start with Mailchimp if: You have a small list (under 1,000), need an all-in-one marketing suite, or want free access to basic email marketing.
  • Switch to ConvertKit if: You’re a creator, blogger, or course seller who values simplicity, automation, and segmentation. ConvertKit scales
