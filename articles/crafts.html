DIY Macrame Wall Hanging: A Beginner’s Guide to Boho Crafts

Looking for a relaxing, creative way to add warmth and texture to your home? Macrame wall hangings are one of the most satisfying crafts you can learn. They require minimal tools, use natural materials, and produce stunning results that look like they came from a boutique. In this DIY tutorial, I’ll walk you through every step to create your own 18-inch macrame wall hanging—from choosing the right cord to mastering the essential knots. Whether you’re a complete beginner or a seasoned crafter, this project will boost your confidence and give you a beautiful piece for your wall.

Why Macrame? The Perfect Craft for Relaxation and Decor

Macrame has made a huge comeback, and for good reason. It’s a mindful, screen-free activity that lets you create something tangible with your hands. Unlike knitting or crochet, macrame uses just your fingers and a few simple knots—no needles required. Plus, the materials are affordable and widely available. This project is ideal for a rainy afternoon, a craft night with friends, or as a handmade gift that shows you care.

Materials You’ll Need

Gather these supplies before you start. Most can be found at craft stores or online. For a beginner-friendly wall hanging, I recommend:

  • Macrame cord: 3-ply single twist cotton cord, 4mm thick. You’ll need about 100 yards total. (Look for “natural cotton macrame cord” at your local craft store.)
  • Wooden dowel or branch: 12 inches long, ½ inch diameter. A smooth dowel works best for beginners.
  • Scissors: Sharp fabric scissors or craft scissors.
  • Measuring tape or ruler: Essential for cutting consistent lengths.
  • Comb or brush: For fraying the ends of the cord.
  • Masking tape or binder clips: To hold your work in place while you knot.

Pro tip: If you’re unsure about color, start with natural beige or white. It hides mistakes well and matches any decor. Once you’re comfortable, experiment with dyed cords for more vibrant crafts.

Safety Tips for Macrame Crafts

While macrame is low-risk, a few precautions will keep your project safe and enjoyable:

  • Protect your surface: Lay down a cutting mat or old towel to avoid scratches from scissors or the dowel.
  • Watch your fingers: When pulling knots tight, avoid pinching your skin. Use your fingertips, not your nails.
  • Work in good light: Knotting can be detailed. Natural daylight or a bright lamp reduces eye strain.
  • Keep scissors away from children: Store sharp tools safely when not in use.
  • Take breaks: Repetitive motions can strain wrists. Stretch your hands every 20 minutes.

Step-by-Step Instructions

Estimated time: 2–3 hours for a complete beginner.

Step 1: Cut Your Cords

You’ll need 24 pieces of cord, each 3.5 yards long (126 inches). That’s the standard length for an 18-inch wall hanging. To cut efficiently, wrap the cord around a 12-inch book or piece of cardboard 24 times, then cut at one end. This gives you equal lengths without measuring each one separately.

Tip: If you want a wider hanging, add more cords. For a narrower one, use 18 cords instead.

Step 2: Attach Cords to the Dowel

Fold each cord in half. Place the looped end over the dowel, then pull the loose ends through the loop. Pull tight to create a “lark’s head knot.” Repeat for all 24 cords, sliding them close together but not overlapping. You should have 48 working ends hanging down.

Secure the dowel horizontally with masking tape to a table or use binder clips to hold it in place. This keeps your work stable while you knot.

Step 3: Learn the Square Knot

The square knot is the backbone of macrame. Here’s how to tie it:

  1. Take four cords (two from one lark’s head, two from the next).
  2. Hold the two outer cords (called working cords) and let the two inner cords (filler cords) hang straight.
  3. Cross the left working cord over the filler cords and under the right working cord.
  4. Bring the right working cord under the filler cords and through the loop on the left side. Pull tight—this is a half knot.
  5. Now cross the right working cord over the filler cords and under the left working cord.
  6. Bring the left working cord under the filler cords and through the loop on the right. Pull tight to complete one square knot.

Practice this knot until it feels natural. It should look like a small, flat square.

Step 4: Create the First Row of Knots

Starting from the left, group the first four cords. Tie one square knot about 1 inch below the dowel. Then move to the next four cords, and so on, across the entire row. You’ll have 12 square knots in total. Keep them evenly spaced—use your ruler to check the distance from the dowel.

Step 5: Add a Second Row (Offset Pattern)

For a classic diamond pattern, offset the second row. Skip the first two cords on the left, then group cords 3–6 (the two right cords from the first knot and the two left cords from the second knot). Tie a square knot about 1.5 inches below the first row. Continue across: cords 7–10, 11–14, and so on. You should have 11 knots in this row. The last two cords on the right will remain unknotted.

Step 6: Continue the Pattern

Repeat steps 4 and 5, alternating rows, until you reach your desired length. For a 12-inch hanging section, aim for 6–8 rows total. Each row should be about 1.5 inches apart. As you work, gently pull knots tight but not so tight that the cords bunch up. The fabric should lie flat.

Step 7: Finish with a Fringe

Once you’re happy with the knotted section, cut the bottom ends of the cords to an even length—about 6–8 inches below the last row. Use a comb or brush to gently fray each cord end. This creates a soft, bohemian fringe. Trim the fringe straight across or in a gentle curve.

Step 8: Hang Your Wall Hanging

Cut a 12-inch piece of cord for a hanging loop. Fold it in half, place the loop over the center of the dowel, and tie the ends in a knot. Alternatively, use a small nail or command hook on the wall. Step back and admire your work!

Practical Tips for Perfecting Your Crafts

  • Keep tension even: Uneven tension makes the pattern look messy. Pull each knot with the same force.
  • Use a board: A corkboard or foam board with pins can hold your work in place, especially for larger projects.
  • Fix mistakes quickly: If you tie a knot wrong, simply untie it with your fingers or a small crochet hook. Cotton cord is forgiving.
  • Add beads: For extra flair, slide wooden beads onto filler cords before tying knots. This is a fun variation for advanced crafts.
  • Wash gently: If the hanging gets dusty, hand wash in cold water with mild soap, then hang to dry. Avoid wringing.

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

  • Cords twisting: If your cords twist, stop and straighten them. Twisted cords create uneven knots.
  • Running out of cord: Always cut a few extra inches per cord. You can trim excess later, but you can’t add length.
  • Uneven rows: Use a ruler to check spacing every few rows. It’s easy to drift without noticing.
