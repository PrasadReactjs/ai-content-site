Welcome to this edition of our newsletter. What is Climate Tech and Why Does It Matter?

Climate tech refers to technologies specifically designed to mitigate or adapt to climate change. This spans renewable energy, electric vehicles, carbon removal, sustainable agriculture, green building materials, and even AI-driven climate modeling. Sustainability, meanwhile, is the broader principle of meeting our present needs without compromising future generations—encompassing everything from corporate ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) goals to everyday lifestyle choices.

The urgency is clear: global CO₂ emissions hit a record high in 2023, and the UN warns we must cut emissions by 45% by 2030 to avoid catastrophic warming. But here’s the good news—investment in climate tech is surging. According to BloombergNEF, global venture capital funding for climate tech reached over $40 billion in 2023, up 25% year-over-year. This is not just an environmental movement; it’s a massive economic shift.

Top Trends in Climate Tech & Sustainability Right Now

1. Renewable Energy Dominance

Solar and wind are now the cheapest sources of electricity in most parts of the world. Innovations like floating solar farms and next-generation wind turbines are pushing boundaries. For instance, the world’s largest offshore wind farm, Dogger Bank in the UK, will power 6 million homes when fully operational.

2. Carbon Capture and Removal (CCR)

While reducing emissions is priority #1, carbon capture is essential for hard-to-abate sectors like cement and steel. Startups like Climeworks and Carbon Engineering are scaling direct air capture (DAC) plants. In 2024, the US Department of Energy announced $1.2 billion for DAC hubs—a huge signal for the industry.

3. Electric Vehicles (EVs) and Battery Storage

EV sales grew 35% globally in 2023, and battery prices have fallen 89% since 2010. But the next frontier is solid-state batteries, which promise faster charging and longer range. Companies like QuantumScape and Toyota are racing to commercialize this tech.

4. Sustainable Agriculture and Food Tech

Food production accounts for ~25% of global emissions. Innovations include lab-grown meat (cultivated meat), precision fermentation for dairy proteins, and regenerative agriculture practices that sequester carbon in soil. Beyond Meat and Impossible Foods are household names, but startups like Upside Foods are now serving cultivated chicken in restaurants.

5. Green Hydrogen

Hydrogen produced using renewable energy (green hydrogen) is being touted as a clean fuel for shipping, aviation, and heavy industry. Projects like the NEOM green hydrogen plant in Saudi Arabia (set to be the world’s largest) are moving from concept to reality.

Practical Tips: How You Can Embrace Climate Tech & Sustainability

Whether you’re an individual, a business owner, or an investor, there are actionable steps you can take right now.

For Individuals:

  • Switch to renewable energy at home. Many utilities offer green power plans. Or install solar panels—costs have dropped 70% in the last decade.
  • Go electric. If you’re in the market for a car, consider an EV. Federal and state incentives can lower the price significantly.
  • Reduce food waste. About one-third of all food produced is wasted. Composting and meal planning can shrink your carbon footprint.
  • Support climate tech startups. Crowdfunding platforms like Wefunder and StartEngine let you invest in early-stage green companies with as little as $100.

For Businesses:

  • Measure your carbon footprint. Use tools like Watershed or Plan A to track Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions.
  • Set science-based targets. Align with the Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) to ensure your goals are credible.
  • Invest in green procurement. Choose suppliers who use renewable energy and sustainable materials.
  • Adopt circular economy principles. Design products for durability, repairability, and recyclability.

For Investors:

  • Look beyond hype. Not all climate tech is created equal. Focus on companies with strong unit economics and real-world deployment.
  • Diversify across sectors. Consider clean energy, energy storage, carbon markets, and sustainable agriculture.
  • Watch for policy tailwinds. The Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) in the US and the EU Green Deal are pouring billions into climate tech—companies that benefit from these policies are well-positioned.

Real-World Success Stories

To inspire you, here are two examples of climate tech making a tangible impact:

  • Ørsted: The Danish energy company transformed from a fossil fuel giant to a renewable energy leader. Today, it’s the world’s largest offshore wind developer and has reduced its carbon emissions by 86% since 2006.
  • Indigo Agriculture: This startup uses data science and microbial seed treatments to help farmers improve crop yields while sequestering carbon in soil. Their program has enrolled millions of acres, paying farmers for carbon credits.

Challenges and What’s Next

Despite the progress, hurdles remain. Scaling up carbon capture is expensive—current costs range from $600 to $1,000 per ton of CO₂. Battery supply chains are still dependent on lithium and cobalt, which raise ethical and environmental concerns. And green hydrogen is not yet cost-competitive with gray hydrogen (made from natural gas).

But the trajectory is clear. The International Energy Agency (IEA) projects that clean energy investment will exceed $2 trillion in 2024, double the amount going to fossil fuels. Governments are setting tougher regulations, and consumers are demanding greener products. The next decade will see climate tech move from early adoption to mainstream ubiquity.
