The Ultimate Guide to Finding the Best Note Taking App in 2025

In a world overflowing with information, the ability to capture, organize, and retrieve your thoughts quickly is no longer a luxury—it's a necessity. Whether you're a busy professional, a student cramming for exams, or a creative brainstorming your next big idea, the best note taking app can transform chaos into clarity. But with dozens of apps vying for your attention, each promising to be the ultimate solution, how do you choose the right one?

After testing over 15 popular applications, comparing their features, pricing, and real-world usability, we’ve narrowed down the top contenders. In this comprehensive guide, we’ll break down the best note taking app options for different needs, complete with detailed pros and cons, pricing breakdowns, and actionable tips to help you make an informed decision.

What Makes the Best Note Taking App?

Before diving into specific apps, it’s important to understand what separates a good tool from a great one. The best note taking app should excel in four key areas:

  • Speed & Simplicity: You should be able to capture a note in under 3 seconds.
  • Organization: Powerful tagging, folders, or linking systems to find notes later.
  • Cross-Platform Sync: Seamless access on your phone, tablet, and computer.
  • Search & Retrieval: Instant search across text, handwriting, and even images.

Your personal workflow will determine which app wins the title of best note taking app for you. Let’s explore the top candidates.

1. Notion – The All-in-One Powerhouse

Best for: Project management, team collaboration, and building custom databases.

Pricing:

  • Free plan: Unlimited pages, blocks, and file uploads (up to 5MB per file).
  • Plus plan: $10/month (billed annually) – unlimited file uploads, 30-day version history.
  • Business plan: $18/month (per user) – advanced permissions, team analytics.

Features

  • Rich text editor with /commands for databases, tables, calendars, and templates.
  • Wiki-style linking between pages (perfect for building a second brain).
  • Powerful relational databases that function like a lightweight spreadsheet.
  • Collaboration features: real-time editing, comments, and task assignments.

Pros

  • Incredible flexibility – you can build anything from a simple to-do list to a full project tracker.
  • Excellent for teams and shared workspaces.
  • Free plan is generous and functional for individual users.

Cons

  • Steep learning curve – beginners often feel overwhelmed by options.
  • Offline mode is limited on mobile.
  • Can become slow with very large databases (500+ pages).

Verdict: If you love customization and need an all-in-one workspace, Notion is arguably the best note taking app for power users. However, for quick, frictionless note-taking, it might feel like overkill.

2. Evernote – The Veteran Workhorse

Best for: Heavy web clipping, archival, and users who need robust OCR (optical character recognition).

Pricing:

  • Free plan: 60MB monthly uploads, sync on up to 2 devices.
  • Personal plan: $10.99/month – 10GB monthly uploads, offline access on mobile.
  • Professional plan: $14.99/month – 20GB uploads, AI-powered search, PDF annotation.

Features

  • Industry-leading web clipper (saves articles, PDFs, and emails flawlessly).
  • Powerful search that recognizes text inside images and handwritten notes.
  • Notebooks and stacks for hierarchical organization.
  • Integration with Google Calendar, Slack, and 100+ other apps.

Pros

  • Excellent search capabilities – finds text in scanned documents and photos.
  • Mature ecosystem with strong third-party integrations.
  • Reliable cross-platform sync (Windows, Mac, iOS, Android, Web).

Cons

  • Free plan is severely limited (only 2 devices, low upload limit).
  • Interface can feel cluttered and dated compared to modern apps.
  • Pricing is higher than many competitors for comparable features.

Verdict: Evernote remains a strong contender for the best note taking app for hoarders and researchers who need to save and search through thousands of web articles. But its restrictive free plan and price hike have pushed many users to alternatives.

3. Obsidian – The Second Brain Builder

Best for: Knowledge management, personal wikis, and linked thinking (Zettelkasten method).

Pricing:

  • Free forever: Core app with unlimited notes and local storage.
  • Sync (optional): $5/month (encrypted, cross-device sync).
  • Publish (optional): $10/month (host your notes as a website).

Features

  • Local-first architecture – your notes are plain Markdown files stored on your device.
  • Bi-directional linking ([[links]]) to connect ideas like a wiki.
  • Graph view to visualize connections between notes.
  • Hundreds of community plugins for customization (Kanban, calendars, mind maps).

Pros

  • Complete privacy – your data is stored locally, not on a cloud server.
  • Extremely fast and responsive, even with thousands of notes.
  • Plugin ecosystem allows you to build exactly the tool you need.

Cons

  • No native mobile app that matches desktop experience (community plugins help).
  • Steep learning curve for non-technical users.
  • Requires manual setup for sync (paid Sync service or third-party like iCloud).

Verdict: For knowledge workers, researchers, and writers who want to build a "second brain," Obsidian is the best note taking app available. The bi-directional linking and graph view are game-changers for connecting ideas.

4. Bear – The Minimalist’s Dream (Apple Only)

Best for: Mac and iOS users who want a beautiful, distraction-free writing experience.

Pricing:

  • Free plan: Basic features, limited to Bear Cloud sync.
  • Pro plan: $2.99/month or $29.99/year – syncs across devices, themes, export options.

Features

  • Elegant, clean interface with focus mode.
  • Advanced Markdown editor with live preview.
  • Powerful hashtag-based organization (#tag/subtag).
  • Export to PDF, Word, HTML, and more.

Pros

  • Stunning design – one of the most beautiful apps on any platform.
  • Incredibly fast and lightweight.
  • Excellent for long-form writing (blog posts, essays, journals).

Cons

  • Only available on Apple devices (no Windows, Android, or Web).
  • Limited organization options (only hashtags, no folders or databases).
  • No web clipper or collaboration features.

Verdict: If you live entirely in the Apple ecosystem and prioritize aesthetics and simplicity, Bear is the best note taking app for you. It’s perfect for writers and journalers who want zero friction.
