The Ultimate Guide to Finding the Best CRM for Nonprofits in 2025

Running a nonprofit is a mission-driven endeavor, but managing donors, volunteers, and communications can quickly become overwhelming without the right tools. A Customer Relationship Management (CRM) system tailored for nonprofits can streamline your operations, deepen supporter relationships, and boost fundraising efforts. But with so many options on the market, how do you choose the best CRM for nonprofits?

In this comprehensive guide, we’ll break down the top CRM platforms for nonprofits, compare their pricing, features, pros and cons, and give you actionable recommendations to help you make the right choice for your organization.

Why Your Nonprofit Needs a Dedicated CRM

A generic CRM might work for a small business, but nonprofits have unique needs: tracking donations, managing volunteers, segmenting supporters by engagement level, and generating tax receipts. A specialized nonprofit CRM automates these tasks, freeing up your team to focus on impact.

Key benefits include:

  • Centralized donor data – No more spreadsheets or siloed information.
  • Automated communications – Send personalized thank-you emails, newsletters, and event invitations.
  • Advanced reporting – Track fundraising progress, donor retention, and campaign ROI.
  • Integration with fundraising tools – Connect with payment processors, email marketing platforms, and event management software.

Top 5 Best CRMs for Nonprofits (Reviewed)

We’ve evaluated the leading nonprofit CRMs based on features, pricing (including free tiers), ease of use, and overall value. Here are our top picks.

1. Salesforce Nonprofit Cloud

Best for: Large nonprofits with complex needs and dedicated IT support.

Overview: Salesforce Nonprofit Cloud is the gold standard for enterprise-level nonprofits. It’s built on the powerful Salesforce platform but offers specialized modules for donor management, program management, and engagement tracking.

Pricing: Salesforce offers 10 free licenses for eligible nonprofits via its Power of Us program. After that, paid plans start at around $25/user/month (discounted for nonprofits). Additional features like Marketing Cloud or AI tools cost extra.

Key Features:

  • Customizable dashboards and reports
  • Donation tracking with recurring gift management
  • Volunteer management module
  • Integration with hundreds of apps (Mailchimp, QuickBooks, etc.)
  • AI-powered insights (Einstein AI)

Pros:

  • Extremely powerful and scalable
  • Free for small teams
  • Vast ecosystem of add-ons and support

Cons:

  • Steep learning curve – requires training
  • Can become expensive as you add users and features
  • Overkill for very small nonprofits

2. Bloomerang

Best for: Mid-sized nonprofits focused on donor retention and relationship building.

Overview: Bloomerang is built with one core mission: helping nonprofits retain donors. Its interface is intuitive, and its analytics are laser-focused on engagement and lifetime value.

Pricing: Starts at $199/month (for up to 1,000 records). Scales up based on contact count. A free trial is available.

Key Features:

  • Donor retention scoring and alerts
  • Automated thank-you and acknowledgment letters
  • Built-in email marketing with templates
  • Event management and ticketing
  • Mobile app for on-the-go access

Pros:

  • User-friendly and easy to set up
  • Excellent donor retention tools
  • Good customer support

Cons:

  • No free tier (except trial)
  • Limited customization compared to Salesforce
  • Reporting can be basic for advanced needs

3. Neon CRM (by Neon One)

Best for: Growing nonprofits that need an all-in-one solution (CRM + fundraising + membership).

Overview: Neon CRM is a robust platform that combines donor management, event registration, membership tracking, and online fundraising in one place.

Pricing: Starts at $89/month (for up to 1,000 contacts). Higher tiers add features like advanced automation and API access. A free demo is available.

Key Features:

  • Online donation forms and peer-to-peer fundraising
  • Membership management with renewals
  • Event registration and ticketing
  • Email marketing and segmentation
  • Customizable dashboards

Pros:

  • Affordable for the feature set
  • Strong membership and event tools
  • Good integration with QuickBooks and Mailchimp

Cons:

  • Interface can feel dated
  • Customer support response times vary
  • Some advanced features require higher-tier plans

4. HubSpot for Nonprofits

Best for: Small to mid-sized nonprofits that want a free, easy-to-start CRM with marketing automation.

Overview: HubSpot offers a free CRM that includes contact management, deal tracking, and basic email tools. Nonprofits can also apply for a discount on paid Marketing, Sales, and Service Hubs.

Pricing: The core CRM is free forever (with limits on storage and automation). Paid hubs start at $20/month (discounted for nonprofits).

Key Features:

  • Contact and company management
  • Email sequencing and templates
  • Live chat and chatbot for websites
  • Basic reporting dashboards
  • Integration with Gmail, Outlook, and 500+ apps

Pros:

  • Generous free tier
  • Easy to use and set up
  • Excellent marketing automation (paid hubs)

Cons:

  • Not built specifically for nonprofits (no donation tracking out of the box)
  • Requires third-party integrations for fundraising
  • Limited customization on free plan

5. Little Green Light

Best for: Small nonprofits and grassroots organizations with tight budgets.

Overview: Little Green Light is a lightweight, affordable CRM that covers the essentials: donor management, gift tracking, and basic reporting.

Pricing: Starts at $39/month (for up to 500 records). The next tier is $59/month for 2,500 records. Annual plans offer discounts.

Key Features:

  • Donor and gift management
  • Constituent segmentation
  • Email integration (via Mailchimp or Constant Contact)
  • Online donation forms (via WePay or Stripe)
  • Custom fields and tags

Pros:

  • Very affordable
  • Simple and intuitive interface
  • Good for basic donor tracking

Cons:

  • Limited automation and reporting
  • No native email marketing (requires integration)
  • Not suitable for complex needs or large databases
